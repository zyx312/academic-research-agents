# Invited Talk Preparation Skill

## Overview

This skill guides academics through preparing and delivering effective presentations for invited talks, keynotes, seminars, and conference presentations. It covers presentation structure, the Assertion-Evidence slide design method, time management, Q&A handling, and strategies for building a speaking portfolio.

## Talk Types and Expectations

### Keynote Address
- **Duration**: 45-60 minutes + 10-15 min Q&A
- **Audience**: Broad conference audience, often interdisciplinary
- **Expectation**: Big-picture vision, field overview, inspiring narrative
- **Preparation time**: 40-80 hours for a polished keynote
- **Tone**: Authoritative yet accessible, forward-looking

### Invited/Plenary Talk
- **Duration**: 30-45 minutes + 5-10 min Q&A
- **Audience**: Conference attendees in your sub-field
- **Expectation**: Recent research contextualized within broader trends
- **Preparation time**: 20-40 hours
- **Tone**: Expert but collegial

### Contributed/Regular Conference Talk
- **Duration**: 15-20 minutes + 3-5 min Q&A
- **Audience**: Specialized session attendees
- **Expectation**: Single study or focused finding
- **Preparation time**: 10-20 hours
- **Tone**: Precise, data-focused

### Departmental Seminar / Colloquium
- **Duration**: 45-60 minutes + 15-30 min Q&A
- **Audience**: Department faculty and students, mixed expertise
- **Expectation**: Research overview with technical depth, potential collaboration signals
- **Preparation time**: 15-30 hours
- **Tone**: Conversational but rigorous

### Industry Talk / Webinar
- **Duration**: 20-45 minutes + Q&A
- **Audience**: Industry professionals, potentially non-academic
- **Expectation**: Practical applications, actionable insights
- **Preparation time**: 10-25 hours
- **Tone**: Practical, solution-oriented, minimize jargon

## Presentation Structure

### The Classic Three-Act Structure

**Act 1: Setup (15-20% of time)**
- Hook: Start with a compelling question, story, surprising fact, or demonstration
- Context: Why this problem matters (real-world relevance)
- Gap: What we did not know or could not do before
- Promise: What this talk will deliver to the audience

**Act 2: Development (60-70% of time)**
- Organize content into 3-5 clear sections (cognitive limit for retention)
- Each section: claim, evidence, implication
- Use transitions to connect sections ("Now that we've seen X, let's consider how Y...")
- Build complexity gradually: simple concepts first, then advanced
- Include one "aha moment" per section

**Act 3: Resolution (10-15% of time)**
- Summarize key takeaways (3-5 bullet points maximum)
- Broader implications and future directions
- End with a memorable closing: call to action, provocative question, or return to the opening story
- Never end with "Thank you" as your last substantive point; make the conclusion itself memorable, then thank the audience

### Alternative Structures

**Problem-Solution-Impact**: Best for applied research or industry talks
- Problem: What challenge exists? (make it vivid)
- Solution: What did you develop/discover?
- Impact: What changed because of your work?

**Journey/Narrative**: Best for keynotes and seminars
- Where we started (historical context or personal story)
- What we discovered along the way (chronological or thematic)
- Where we are now and where we are heading

**Modular**: Best for diverse audiences
- Self-contained sections that can be expanded or compressed
- Allows real-time adjustment based on audience engagement
- Each module works independently

## Slide Design: The Assertion-Evidence Method

### Core Principles

Traditional slides use topic headlines (e.g., "Experimental Results") and bullet points. The Assertion-Evidence method replaces these with:

- **Headline**: A complete sentence stating the slide's key assertion (the takeaway)
- **Body**: Visual evidence supporting the assertion (images, graphs, diagrams)
- **No bullet points**: Visual evidence replaces text lists

### Example Comparison

**Traditional slide:**
- Title: "Performance Results"
- Bullets: Algorithm A achieved 95% accuracy; Algorithm B achieved 87% accuracy; Our method outperforms existing approaches

**Assertion-Evidence slide:**
- Headline: "Our algorithm achieves 95% accuracy, outperforming the best existing method by 8 percentage points"
- Body: A clear bar chart comparing all methods with confidence intervals

### Design Guidelines

- **Font**: Sans-serif (Calibri, Arial, Helvetica), minimum 24pt for body text
- **Headline**: 28-32pt, bold, limited to 2 lines
- **Colors**: High contrast, limited palette (2-3 colors plus grayscale)
- **Images**: High resolution, fill the slide body, use professional diagrams
- **Data visualization**: Clean axes, labeled clearly, highlight the key comparison
- **Animation**: Use sparingly and purposefully (progressive reveal for complex figures)
- **Slide count**: Roughly 1 slide per minute of presentation (adjust for complexity)

### Slide Types to Include

1. **Title slide**: Your name, affiliation, talk title, date, event
2. **Outline/roadmap slide**: Show structure (3-5 sections), revisit as transitions
3. **Context/motivation slides**: Why this matters
4. **Method slides**: What you did (keep simple, offer details in backup slides)
5. **Results slides**: Data with clear assertion headlines
6. **Summary slide**: 3-5 key takeaways
7. **Acknowledgment slide**: Funding, collaborators, students
8. **Backup slides**: Detailed methods, additional data, anticipated Q&A answers

## Time Management

### Preparation Timeline

**4-6 weeks before:**
- Confirm topic, duration, audience, and technical setup
- Outline the talk structure and key messages
- Begin slide design for core content

**2-3 weeks before:**
- Complete first draft of all slides
- Practice talk once (time it, identify problem areas)
- Get feedback from a colleague or lab member

**1 week before:**
- Refine slides based on feedback
- Practice 2-3 more times (aim for +-2 minutes of target)
- Prepare backup slides for anticipated questions
- Test any demos or videos

**Day before:**
- Final practice run
- Check equipment compatibility (projector, cables, clicker)
- Prepare a printed outline as a safety net
- Rest and hydrate

### During the Talk

- **Pacing rule**: If halfway through time, be halfway through content
- **Cut plan**: Know which slides or sections to skip if running long
- **Time signals**: Place subtle time markers on slides (e.g., "15 min" in speaker notes)
- **Never rush the conclusion**: Cut middle content to protect the ending

## Handling Q&A

### Preparation
- Anticipate 5-10 likely questions and prepare concise answers
- Create backup slides addressing common follow-up topics
- Practice bridging from difficult questions to your key messages

### During Q&A
- Listen to the complete question before responding
- Repeat or paraphrase the question (ensures everyone heard it, buys thinking time)
- Be concise: aim for 30-60 second answers
- It is acceptable to say "That's a great question that I don't have a complete answer to"
- For hostile or off-topic questions: acknowledge, briefly address, offer to discuss offline

### Difficult Question Types

- **"Have you considered X?"**: "We have explored that direction and found [Y]. Happy to discuss further."
- **"This contradicts [other work]"**: "That's an important point. The key difference is [specific factor]."
- **"What about [limitation]?"**: Acknowledge honestly, explain what you plan to do about it
- **"I disagree with your approach"**: "I appreciate that perspective. Could you elaborate on what alternative you would suggest?"

## Virtual Presentation Tips

- **Camera**: Position at eye level, look at the camera (not the screen) when speaking
- **Lighting**: Face a window or use a ring light; avoid backlighting
- **Audio**: Use a dedicated microphone (headset or USB condenser)
- **Background**: Clean, professional, or use a subtle virtual background
- **Engagement**: Use polls, chat prompts, and direct questions more frequently
- **Backup plan**: Have slides as PDF in case screen sharing fails
- **Dual monitor**: Present on one screen, monitor chat/notes on another
- **Energy**: Speak 10-15% more energetically than in person to compensate for screen distance

## Building a Speaking Portfolio

### How to Get Invited

- **Publish visible work**: High-impact papers attract invitations naturally
- **Attend and engage at conferences**: Ask good questions, introduce yourself to organizers
- **Volunteer**: Organize sessions, serve on committees, chair panels
- **Network strategically**: Colleagues who see you present well will recommend you
- **Share talks online**: Post recordings on YouTube or your personal website
- **Propose workshops or tutorials**: Lower barrier than invited talks, builds credibility
- **Ask**: Directly offer to give talks at partner institutions and groups

### Tracking and Marketing Your Talks
- Maintain a talks page on your personal website (title, venue, date, slides/recording)
- Include talks in your CV under "Invited Presentations" and "Conference Presentations"
- Share talk summaries on LinkedIn and WeChat after presenting
- Request feedback from hosts to improve continuously

## Cultural Considerations for International Talks

- **Language**: Practice pronunciation of key technical terms; speak slightly slower for multilingual audiences
- **Humor**: Use universal humor (situational, self-deprecating) rather than cultural references
- **Formality**: Match the expected level (more formal in Asia and Europe, less formal in US/Australia)
- **Acknowledgments**: In Chinese academic culture, acknowledge seniors, mentors, and institutions prominently
- **Time zones**: For virtual talks across time zones, confirm the time in the audience's local time
- **Q&A culture**: Some audiences ask questions during the talk, others wait until the end; ask the host about norms

## Prompt Templates

### Prepare a Talk Outline

```
Help me prepare an invited talk:
- Event: [conference/seminar name]
- Talk type: [keynote/invited/contributed/seminar]
- Duration: [minutes for talk] + [minutes for Q&A]
- Audience: [expertise level, backgrounds, size]
- Topic: [research area or specific paper]
- Key message: [one sentence I want the audience to remember]

Please create:
1. A three-act structure outline with timing for each section
2. 3-5 key assertions for the main content sections
3. A compelling opening hook (3 options to choose from)
4. A memorable closing (3 options)
5. A list of 5-10 anticipated Q&A questions with suggested answers
6. A suggested slide outline with Assertion-Evidence headlines
```

### Design Slides Using Assertion-Evidence Method

```
Convert these traditional bullet-point slides into Assertion-Evidence format:
[paste slide content or outline]

For each slide, provide:
1. A sentence headline stating the key assertion (max 2 lines)
2. Description of the visual evidence to use (chart type, diagram, image)
3. Any text that should appear on the slide besides the headline
4. Speaker notes for what to say while showing this slide
5. Transition sentence to the next slide
```

### Prepare for Q&A

```
I am giving a talk on [topic] at [event]. My main findings are:
[list key findings]

Known limitations of my work include:
[list limitations]

Controversial aspects might include:
[list any controversial points]

Please:
1. Generate 10 likely audience questions (mix of friendly, challenging, and hostile)
2. Draft concise answers for each (30-60 seconds when spoken)
3. Suggest 3 bridging phrases for redirecting difficult questions
4. Recommend 3-5 backup slides I should prepare
5. Identify the single most difficult question I might face and help me prepare for it
```

### Adapt a Talk for a Different Audience

```
I have an existing talk designed for [original audience]:
[paste outline or key points]

I need to adapt it for [new audience]:
- New audience expertise: [level]
- New audience interests: [what they care about]
- New duration: [minutes]
- New context: [industry event, different discipline, public lecture, etc.]

Please:
1. Identify which sections to keep, modify, or remove
2. Suggest new framing and examples for the target audience
3. Adjust the level of technical detail
4. Propose a new opening hook appropriate for this audience
5. Recommend changes to the conclusion and call to action
```
