# Review Writing

## Overview

Draft constructive peer reviews for journal and conference submissions. Provides structured
evaluation covering novelty, methodology, experiments, writing quality, and overall
recommendation. Follows best practices for fair, ethical, and actionable reviewing.

## Reviewer Responsibilities and Ethics

### Core Responsibilities

1. **Timeliness**: Complete reviews within the agreed deadline (typically 2-4 weeks).
   If you cannot meet the deadline, notify the editor immediately.
2. **Confidentiality**: Treat the manuscript as confidential. Do not share it, discuss
   its contents with others, or use unpublished findings in your own work.
3. **Objectivity**: Evaluate the work on its scientific merit, not on the authors'
   identity, institution, nationality, or gender. Decline reviews where you have
   conflicts of interest.
4. **Thoroughness**: Read the entire manuscript carefully, including supplementary
   materials. Do not skim and guess.
5. **Constructiveness**: Your goal is to help improve the work, not to demonstrate
   superiority. Every criticism should be accompanied by a suggestion.

### Ethical Red Lines

- Never plagiarize ideas from manuscripts you review.
- Disclose conflicts of interest to the editor (collaborators, competitors, same
  institution, financial interests).
- If you suspect misconduct (data fabrication, plagiarism, dual submission), report
  to the editor in confidential comments -- do not accuse the authors directly.
- Do not agree to review papers outside your expertise just to build your review record.

### When to Decline a Review

- The topic falls outside your area of competence.
- You have a conflict of interest with the authors.
- You cannot complete the review by the deadline.
- You have reviewed the same manuscript for another venue recently.

## Structured Review Template

### Section 1: Summary of the Paper (3-5 sentences)

Demonstrate that you understood the paper. State the research question, the approach
taken, and the key findings. This helps authors confirm you engaged with their work
and helps the editor when the review is ambiguous.

### Section 2: Overall Assessment (1-2 paragraphs)

Provide your high-level evaluation. Is this an important contribution? Does it advance
the state of the art? Is the methodology sound? This section frames everything that
follows.

### Section 3: Major Strengths (numbered list)

Identify 2-5 significant positive aspects:
- Novelty of the approach or formulation
- Rigor of the experimental design
- Practical significance or real-world applicability
- Clarity and quality of presentation
- Comprehensive comparison with prior work

### Section 4: Major Weaknesses (numbered list)

Identify substantive issues that must be addressed:
- Methodological flaws or unjustified assumptions
- Missing baselines or comparisons
- Insufficient experimental evidence for claims
- Lack of statistical rigor (no confidence intervals, no significance tests)
- Overclaiming or unsupported conclusions
- Missing related work that challenges the claims

For each weakness, explain WHY it is a problem and SUGGEST how to fix it.

### Section 5: Minor Comments (numbered list)

Smaller issues that improve quality but are not deal-breakers:
- Typographical or grammatical errors (cite specific locations)
- Figure quality or readability issues
- Missing references
- Notation inconsistencies
- Suggestions for additional experiments that would strengthen (but are not essential)

### Section 6: Questions to the Authors

List specific questions you want addressed in a revision:
- Clarification of ambiguous statements
- Requests for additional analysis or ablations
- Questions about reproducibility (code, data availability)

### Section 7: Confidential Comments to the Editor

Content NOT shared with authors. Use this for:
- Your overall recommendation and confidence level
- Concerns about ethics or misconduct
- Conflict of interest disclosures
- Comments about whether the paper fits the venue scope
- Comparison with other papers you are reviewing in the same batch

## Evaluating Key Dimensions

### Methodology Assessment

- Is the problem formulation rigorous and well-defined?
- Are assumptions stated explicitly and justified?
- Is the approach technically correct? Check derivations and proofs.
- Are there alternative approaches that should have been considered?
- Is the experimental setup appropriate for the claims being made?
- Are there confounding variables that are not controlled?

### Novelty Assessment

- What is genuinely new compared to existing work?
- Is the novelty incremental (small improvement) or conceptual (new paradigm)?
- Does the paper clearly articulate its contribution beyond prior art?
- Are the differences from closely related work substantial enough?

### Significance Assessment

- Does this work solve an important problem?
- Will other researchers build on this work?
- Does it have practical impact beyond the academic community?
- Is the magnitude of improvement meaningful (not just statistically significant)?

### Experimental Rigor

- Are experiments reproducible from the description given?
- Are datasets described adequately (size, splits, preprocessing)?
- Are baselines fair and up-to-date?
- Are results averaged over multiple runs with variance reported?
- Are ablation studies included to justify design choices?
- Is the evaluation metric appropriate for the task?

### Writing Quality

- Is the paper well-organized with a logical flow?
- Are figures and tables informative and well-designed?
- Is notation consistent and clearly defined?
- Is the abstract accurate and self-contained?
- Is related work discussed fairly and comprehensively?

## Constructive Criticism Techniques

### The Sandwich Approach

Frame critical feedback between acknowledgment and suggestion:
- "The authors propose an interesting approach to X. However, the evaluation on Y
  is insufficient because Z. I suggest the authors add experiments on W to
  strengthen this contribution."

### Be Specific, Not Vague

Bad: "The experiments are weak."
Good: "Table 2 compares against only two baselines from 2019. The authors should
include comparisons with [Specific Method A, 2023] and [Specific Method B, 2024],
which report results on the same benchmark."

### Separate Observation from Judgment

Bad: "This paper is poorly written."
Good: "Section 3.2 introduces notation (alpha, beta, gamma) that is not defined until
Section 4.1, making the derivation difficult to follow on first reading."

### Distinguish Mandatory from Optional

Clearly label which issues MUST be addressed for acceptance versus which are
suggestions for improvement. This helps both authors and editors.

## Recommendation Guidelines

### Strong Accept
- Novel, significant contribution with rigorous methodology
- Well-written and complete as-is
- Among the top papers you have reviewed recently

### Accept / Weak Accept
- Solid contribution with sound methodology
- Minor issues that can be addressed in camera-ready
- Clearly above the acceptance threshold

### Borderline / Major Revision
- Interesting idea but execution has notable gaps
- Fixable issues that require substantial additional work
- Outcome depends on quality of revisions

### Reject / Weak Reject
- Fundamental methodological flaws
- Insufficient novelty over existing work
- Claims not supported by evidence
- Not appropriate for the venue

### Strong Reject
- Serious ethical concerns
- Technically incorrect core contribution
- Significant overlap with published work

## Review Timeline Management

| Phase | Duration | Activities |
|-------|----------|------------|
| Initial read | Day 1-2 | Skim for scope, note first impressions |
| Deep read | Day 3-7 | Detailed analysis, check references, verify claims |
| Draft review | Day 8-10 | Write structured review following template |
| Cool-down | Day 11-12 | Set aside, then re-read with fresh eyes |
| Finalize | Day 13-14 | Polish language, ensure tone is constructive |

### Tips for Efficiency

- Take notes during your first reading -- your initial reactions are valuable.
- Use a checklist to ensure you cover all evaluation dimensions.
- If a paper is clearly below threshold, you still owe the authors a respectful
  and specific review explaining why.
- For revised manuscripts, focus on whether specific concerns were addressed.

## Conference vs. Journal Review Differences

### Conference Reviews
- Typically shorter (1-2 pages)
- Binary outcome (accept/reject) -- no revision cycle
- Shorter timeline (2-3 weeks)
- Often include a confidence score
- Rebuttal may be allowed before final decision

### Journal Reviews
- More detailed (2-5 pages)
- Multiple revision rounds possible
- Longer timeline (4-8 weeks)
- Should explicitly state conditions for acceptance
- May request specific additional experiments

## Common Pitfalls to Avoid

1. **Reviewing the paper you wish the authors had written** instead of the paper
   they actually wrote.
2. **Requesting unnecessary experiments** that do not address actual weaknesses.
3. **Being harsh to reject and lenient to accept** based on author prestige.
4. **Ignoring supplementary materials** that address concerns in the main text.
5. **Providing one-line reviews** that give no actionable feedback.
6. **Letting personal taste override objective evaluation** of scientific merit.

## Prompt Templates for Claude

### Generate a Structured Peer Review

```
You are an experienced peer reviewer in [FIELD]. Review the following manuscript
using this structure:

1. SUMMARY (3-5 sentences): Summarize the research question, approach, and findings.
2. OVERALL ASSESSMENT (1 paragraph): High-level evaluation of contribution.
3. MAJOR STRENGTHS (numbered, 2-5 items): What the paper does well.
4. MAJOR WEAKNESSES (numbered, 2-5 items): Substantive issues. For each, explain
   the problem and suggest a fix.
5. MINOR COMMENTS (numbered): Smaller issues for improvement.
6. QUESTIONS TO AUTHORS: Specific clarifications needed.
7. RECOMMENDATION: [Strong Accept / Accept / Major Revision / Reject] with justification.

Manuscript details:
- Title: [TITLE]
- Venue: [JOURNAL/CONFERENCE]
- Field: [FIELD]

[PASTE MANUSCRIPT TEXT OR KEY SECTIONS]
```

### Evaluate Methodology Rigor

```
Analyze the methodology section of this paper for rigor and completeness:

1. Are all assumptions explicitly stated and justified?
2. Is the experimental design appropriate for the research questions?
3. Are there confounding variables not accounted for?
4. Are baselines fair, recent, and comprehensive?
5. Is the evaluation metric appropriate?
6. Can the experiments be reproduced from the description?
7. Are results statistically validated (confidence intervals, significance tests)?

Methodology section:
[PASTE METHODOLOGY]
```

### Draft Confidential Comments to Editor

```
Based on the following review, draft confidential comments to the editor. Include:
1. Your overall recommendation and confidence level (1-5 scale).
2. Whether the paper fits the venue scope.
3. Any ethical concerns.
4. How this paper compares to others in the current review batch (if applicable).
5. Specific conditions that must be met for acceptance.

Review content:
[PASTE YOUR REVIEW DRAFT]
```

### Convert Harsh Review to Constructive Feedback

```
Rewrite the following review comments to be more constructive while preserving the
technical substance. For each criticism:
- Acknowledge what the authors attempted
- Explain clearly why it is a problem
- Suggest a specific path to improvement

Original comments:
[PASTE HARSH COMMENTS]
```
