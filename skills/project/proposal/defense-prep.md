# Proposal Defense Preparation

## Overview

Prepare for research proposal defense presentations, including NSFC panel reviews,
thesis proposal defenses, project mid-term evaluations, and project acceptance reviews.
Covers slide design, presentation delivery, question anticipation, response strategies,
and rehearsal methods. A well-prepared defense can turn a borderline proposal into a
funded project.

## Defense Presentation Structure

### The 10-15 Minute Framework

Most proposal defenses allow 10-15 minutes for presentation plus 5-15 minutes for
Q&A. Every second counts. Use this allocation:

| Segment | Duration (12 min) | Duration (15 min) | Content |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|---------|
| Opening Hook | 1 min | 1 min | Problem motivation, why it matters now |
| Background | 2 min | 2.5 min | State of art, gap identification |
| Objectives | 1 min | 1.5 min | Clear research questions and goals |
| Methodology | 4 min | 5 min | Technical approach, key innovations |
| Preliminary Results | 2 min | 2.5 min | Evidence of feasibility |
| Expected Outcomes | 1 min | 1.5 min | Deliverables and impact |
| Summary | 1 min | 1 min | Key takeaways, call to action |

### Opening: The First 60 Seconds

The opening determines whether the panel will engage with your presentation or
tune out. Strategies for a strong opening:

1. **Start with a concrete problem scenario**: "Every year, X thousand accidents
   occur because of Y. Current systems fail to detect Z in adverse conditions."
2. **Use a striking statistic**: "Despite billions invested in autonomous driving,
   perception systems still fail in rain at 3x the rate of clear conditions."
3. **Show a compelling visual**: A failure case, a gap in existing solutions, or
   a motivating real-world example.
4. **State your thesis clearly**: "This project will develop X to achieve Y,
   enabling Z for the first time."

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not start with "Today I will present..." -- jump straight into content.
- Do not begin with a lengthy personal introduction.
- Do not open with dense equations or technical definitions.
- Do not read your slides word for word.

### Body: Building the Narrative

Follow the problem-solution-evidence arc:

1. **Problem and gap** (Background slides):
   - What is the challenge? Use concrete examples.
   - What have others tried? Show you know the field.
   - What is missing? Clearly identify the specific gap.

2. **Your approach** (Methodology slides):
   - Present your solution conceptually first, then in detail.
   - Use a system overview diagram on one slide.
   - Then drill into key innovations (1-2 slides per innovation point).
   - Show how tasks connect (do not present them as isolated activities).

3. **Evidence of feasibility** (Preliminary results slides):
   - Present preliminary data, pilot studies, or proof-of-concept results.
   - Compare with baselines to show your approach works.
   - Be honest about limitations -- panels respect intellectual honesty.

### Closing: The Last 60 Seconds

- Summarize 3-4 key takeaways (not a repeat of the outline).
- Restate the significance of the expected outcomes.
- End with a forward-looking statement about impact.
- Thank the panel and invite questions.

## Slide Design Principles

### Content Rules

1. **One message per slide**: Each slide should convey exactly one idea.
2. **Maximum 6 lines of text**: If you need more, split into two slides.
3. **Figures over text**: A well-designed diagram communicates more than
   a bullet list.
4. **Consistent visual language**: Use the same color scheme, fonts, and
   layout throughout.
5. **Label everything**: Axis labels, units, legends on all figures and charts.

### Visual Design

- **Font size**: Minimum 24pt for body text, 28pt for titles. If the panel
  cannot read it from the back of the room, it is too small.
- **Color**: Use high contrast (dark text on light background). Avoid red-green
  combinations (color blindness). Use color purposefully to highlight key points.
- **White space**: Do not fill every pixel. Empty space helps guide the eye.
- **Animations**: Use sparingly and only when they serve a purpose (revealing
  steps in a process, building complexity gradually).

### Slide Types to Include

1. **Title slide**: Project title, your name, affiliation, date.
2. **Motivation slide**: The problem in concrete terms, with a visual.
3. **Literature landscape**: A structured overview of prior work (table or
   taxonomy, not a wall of text).
4. **Gap identification**: What is missing (transition from background to
   your contribution).
5. **Objective slide**: Clear, numbered objectives.
6. **System overview**: One figure showing your entire approach.
7. **Technical detail slides**: 2-3 slides on key innovations.
8. **Preliminary results**: Data, comparisons, benchmarks.
9. **Work plan**: Timeline or Gantt chart.
10. **Expected outcomes**: Deliverables and impact.
11. **Summary slide**: 3-4 bullet takeaways (NOT an outline repetition).

### Number of Slides

Rule of thumb: 1 slide per minute of presentation time.
- 10-minute talk: 10-12 slides (main deck)
- 15-minute talk: 14-16 slides (main deck)
- Plus 5-10 backup slides for Q&A

## Anticipating Questions

### Common Question Categories

#### 1. Methodology Questions

- "Why did you choose method X instead of method Y?"
- "How do you handle edge case Z?"
- "What are the assumptions underlying your approach?"
- "How will you validate that your method works?"
- "What is the computational complexity of your approach?"

**Preparation:** For every methodological choice, prepare a 30-second justification
explaining why you chose this approach and why alternatives are less suitable.

#### 2. Novelty and Contribution Questions

- "How is this different from [similar work by Author X]?"
- "What is the key innovation compared to existing approaches?"
- "Isn't this just an incremental improvement over [method Y]?"
- "What is the theoretical contribution beyond engineering?"

**Preparation:** Prepare a comparison table (in backup slides) clearly showing
differences from the 3-5 most similar works.

#### 3. Feasibility Questions

- "Do you have preliminary results supporting this approach?"
- "How realistic is this timeline?"
- "Do you have access to the necessary data/equipment?"
- "What happens if [key assumption] does not hold?"
- "What is your backup plan if approach X fails?"

**Preparation:** Have concrete evidence ready. Data plots, preliminary experiment
results, or at minimum, evidence from related work that your approach is viable.

#### 4. Impact and Significance Questions

- "Who will benefit from this research?"
- "What is the practical application?"
- "How does this align with [funder's strategic priorities]?"
- "What is the broader impact beyond your specific application?"

**Preparation:** Prepare 2-3 concrete use cases and quantifiable impact estimates.

#### 5. Budget and Resource Questions

- "Why do you need [expensive equipment X]?"
- "Is [budget item Y] really necessary?"
- "How will the graduate students be supervised?"
- "What percentage of your time will you dedicate to this project?"

**Preparation:** Be ready to justify every significant budget item and explain
your time commitment.

#### 6. Critical / Challenging Questions

- "Your preliminary results show only X% improvement -- is that meaningful?"
- "Reference [Z] already solved this problem in 2023."
- "The scope seems too ambitious for the proposed budget/timeline."

**Preparation:** These require honest, thoughtful responses. See the section
on handling difficult questions below.

## Handling Difficult Questions

### General Strategy: The ACE Framework

- **Acknowledge**: Show you understand the question and its significance.
  "That is an excellent point. Indeed, [restate the concern]."
- **Clarify**: Address the specific concern with evidence or reasoning.
  "Our approach differs from [reference] in that..."
- **Extend**: Connect to the broader narrative.
  "This is exactly why we included [task X] in our methodology to address..."

### When You Do Not Know the Answer

It is acceptable -- and sometimes preferable -- to say you do not know, rather
than to fabricate an answer that an expert panel will see through.

Good responses:
- "That is a great question that I have not fully explored yet. My initial
  thinking is [hypothesis], and we plan to investigate this in [Phase X]."
- "I do not have data on that specific point, but based on [related evidence],
  I would expect [reasonable estimate]."
- "That is an interesting direction I had not considered. I will look into it."

Bad responses:
- Making up data or results.
- Becoming defensive or argumentative.
- Giving a long, rambling non-answer.
- Saying "I don't know" and stopping -- always add what you would do to find out.

### When the Question Challenges Your Core Approach

1. Listen fully without interrupting.
2. Take a breath before responding.
3. Acknowledge the validity of the concern.
4. Explain your reasoning for the chosen approach.
5. If the concern has merit, acknowledge it and explain how you would address it.
6. Reference backup slides if you have relevant data.

### When Two Panel Members Disagree

- Do not take sides.
- Acknowledge both perspectives.
- Explain how your approach can accommodate or test both viewpoints.
- Move on gracefully if the debate is between panelists.

## Practice Methods

### Solo Rehearsal

1. **Timed run-through**: Practice with a timer. If you go over, cut content --
   do not speed up your delivery.
2. **Record yourself**: Video review reveals habits you do not notice in real time
   (filler words, looking at the screen, fidgeting).
3. **Slide-free practice**: Can you explain your project in 5 minutes without
   any slides? If not, you do not know it well enough.

### Mock Defense

1. Recruit 2-3 colleagues, ideally including at least one person outside your
   immediate research area.
2. Give the full presentation under time constraints.
3. Have them ask challenging questions (provide them with question categories
   in advance).
4. Debrief: What was unclear? What questions were hardest? Where did you
   lose their attention?

### Question Practice

1. Write down 20 potential questions across all categories.
2. Prepare 30-second responses for each.
3. Practice delivering responses out loud (not just in your head).
4. Have a colleague ask them in random order to simulate pressure.

### Timing Practice

- Practice cutting your talk to 80% of the allotted time to build in buffer
  for unexpected delays (technical issues, panel interruptions).
- Identify 2-3 "cuttable" slides that can be skipped if you are running long.
- Know exactly which slides you can speed through and which need full delivery.

## Body Language and Delivery

### Posture and Presence

- Stand upright with weight evenly distributed.
- Face the audience, not the screen.
- Use the screen for reference but talk to the panel.
- Move naturally -- do not stand rigidly or pace nervously.

### Voice

- Speak slowly and clearly (slower than you think is necessary).
- Vary your tone -- monotone delivery loses audiences quickly.
- Pause after key points to let them sink in.
- Project your voice to the back of the room (or speak clearly into the microphone).

### Eye Contact

- Make eye contact with different panel members throughout.
- When answering a question, look at the person who asked.
- Do not stare at one person the entire time.

### Pointer and Gestures

- Use a laser pointer or cursor to guide attention on complex figures.
- Gesture with open hands to emphasize points.
- Avoid nervous habits (clicking pen, touching face, hands in pockets).

## Backup Slides Strategy

Prepare 5-10 backup slides placed after your conclusion slide. These are
not presented unless needed during Q&A.

### Essential Backup Slides

1. **Detailed methodology**: Technical details you cut from the main talk.
2. **Additional preliminary results**: Extra data supporting feasibility.
3. **Comparison with related work**: Detailed comparison table.
4. **Risk mitigation table**: Specific risks and contingency plans.
5. **Detailed budget breakdown**: Line items with justification.
6. **Extended literature review**: Additional references and context.
7. **Alternative approaches considered**: Why you chose A over B and C.
8. **Detailed timeline**: Full Gantt chart with all subtasks.

### Organizing Backup Slides

- Group by topic (methodology, results, budget, literature).
- Number them clearly (Backup-1, Backup-2, etc.) or title descriptively.
- Know which slide answers which question -- prepare a mental index.
- Practice navigating to specific backup slides quickly (know the slide numbers).

## Defense Context Variations

### NSFC Panel Review (基金委会评)

- Typically 12-15 minutes presentation + 5-10 minutes Q&A.
- Panel of 7-15 experts, not all in your exact subfield.
- Focus on scientific innovation, research basis, and feasibility.
- Be prepared for questions about how this differs from other applicants'
  proposals (the panel reviews many proposals in your area).

### Thesis Proposal Defense (开题报告答辩)

- Typically 15-20 minutes presentation + 10-15 minutes Q&A.
- Committee of 5-7 faculty members.
- Focus on completeness of literature review, clarity of research questions,
  and feasibility of the methodology.
- Panel expects to see a detailed research plan and timeline for completion.

### Project Mid-Term Review (中期检查)

- Report on progress against original plan.
- Explain any deviations and why.
- Present preliminary results and revised timeline.
- Be honest about challenges -- reviewers can tell when you are hiding problems.

### Project Acceptance Review (结题验收)

- Demonstrate that deliverables have been met.
- Present key results and their significance.
- Show publications, patents, and trained students.
- Discuss future directions and sustainability.

## Prompt Templates for Claude

### Generate Defense Slide Outline

```
Create a slide-by-slide outline for a [TIME]-minute proposal defense presentation:

Project title: [TITLE]
Funding body: [FUNDER]
Research topic: [TOPIC]
Key innovations: [LIST 3-4 INNOVATION POINTS]
Preliminary results available: [DESCRIBE]

Generate:
1. Title and content for each slide (1 slide per minute)
2. Speaker notes for key slides (what to say, not what to show)
3. Transitions between slides
4. 5-8 backup slide topics
5. Timing allocation for each slide
```

### Prepare Anticipated Questions and Answers

```
Based on the following research proposal, generate 15-20 likely questions that
a review panel might ask, organized by category:

Proposal summary:
[PASTE KEY SECTIONS OF PROPOSAL]

For each question:
1. State the question
2. Explain why a reviewer might ask it (what concern it addresses)
3. Provide a concise, well-structured answer (30-60 seconds when spoken)
4. Suggest a backup slide topic if relevant

Categories to cover:
- Methodology choices and alternatives
- Novelty and contribution
- Feasibility and preliminary results
- Impact and significance
- Budget and resources
- Timeline and risk management
```

### Create Comparison Table with Related Work

```
Create a comparison table for a defense backup slide that distinguishes my
approach from related work:

My approach: [DESCRIPTION]
Related works:
1. [AUTHOR/YEAR: BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
2. [AUTHOR/YEAR: BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
3. [AUTHOR/YEAR: BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
4. [AUTHOR/YEAR: BRIEF DESCRIPTION]

Comparison dimensions:
- [DIMENSION 1, e.g., Methodology]
- [DIMENSION 2, e.g., Data requirements]
- [DIMENSION 3, e.g., Performance]
- [DIMENSION 4, e.g., Applicability]

Format as a clean table suitable for a presentation slide. Highlight where
my approach is superior or novel.
```

### Practice Mock Q&A Scenarios

```
Act as a skeptical but fair review panel member for my proposal defense.
Ask me one challenging question at a time about the following proposal:

[PASTE PROPOSAL SUMMARY OR KEY DETAILS]

After I respond, evaluate my answer on:
1. Did I address the concern directly?
2. Was my response concise enough (under 60 seconds)?
3. Did I provide evidence or reasoning?
4. What could I improve?

Then ask the next question. Continue for 10 questions across different categories
(methodology, novelty, feasibility, impact, budget).
```

### Polish Speaker Notes

```
Write speaker notes for the following presentation slides. For each slide:
- Write exactly what the presenter should SAY (conversational tone, not
  reading bullet points)
- Include timing guidance
- Mark emphasis points and pause moments
- Suggest transitions to the next slide

Slides:
[PASTE SLIDE CONTENT OR TITLES WITH KEY POINTS]

Target audience: [REVIEW PANEL TYPE]
Total presentation time: [MINUTES]
```

## Recommended Resources for Academic Presentation Skills

### Essential Reading

The following resources are widely regarded as the best guides for improving
academic presentation skills. Read them before your first major defense.

**Jonathan Shewchuk — "Giving an Academic Talk"**
- Comprehensive guide covering slide design, delivery, and audience engagement.
- Emphasizes the importance of telling a story rather than listing results.
- Key takeaway: every slide should convey exactly one idea; if you need a
  bullet list of more than 4 items, you have too much on the slide.
- Includes specific advice for different talk lengths (5-minute lightning talks
  to 45-minute invited lectures).

**Michael Ernst — "How to Give a Talk"**
- Practical guide from a computer science perspective, applicable across fields.
- Covers common mistakes and how to avoid them.
- Emphasizes practice and iteration: give the talk at least 3 times before
  the real presentation.
- Key takeaway: the purpose of a talk is not to present results but to
  stimulate interest in your work; the audience should leave wanting to
  read your paper.

**Simon Peyton Jones — "How to Give a Great Research Talk"**
- Video and written guide focused on making technical content accessible.
- The "one message per talk" principle: identify the single key idea you
  want the audience to remember, and build everything around it.
- Practical advice on handling questions and managing nervousness.

**Patrick Winston — "How to Speak" (MIT lecture)**
- Classic lecture on public speaking for academics and engineers.
- Covers the "Promise-Delivery" framework for structuring talks.
- Discusses voice modulation, pacing, and physical presence.
- Available as a recorded lecture; watch it at least once.

### Presentation Frameworks

**The Assertion-Evidence Framework (Michael Alley)**
- Replace bullet-point slides with assertion-based slides.
- Each slide has a sentence-length assertion as the title (not a topic phrase).
- The body of the slide provides visual evidence supporting the assertion.
- Research shows this format improves audience comprehension and retention.
- Example: Instead of title "Sensor Fusion Results", use "Multi-modal fusion
  reduces false positives by 40% compared to camera-only detection."

**The Lessig Method (inspired by Lawrence Lessig)**
- Very high slide count with minimal text per slide (1-5 words).
- Each slide supports a single spoken phrase.
- Creates a cinematic flow; excellent for storytelling-heavy presentations.
- Not suitable for all academic contexts but powerful for keynotes and
  public lectures.

### Rehearsal Resources

- **TED Talk guidelines**: Study TED speakers' techniques for audience engagement
- **Toastmasters International**: Practice environment for developing speaking skills
- **University writing centers**: Many offer presentation coaching in addition to writing support
- **Lab presentation sessions**: Regular practice presentations within your research group
