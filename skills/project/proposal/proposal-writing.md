# Proposal Writing (General)

## Overview

A universal research proposal writing framework applicable across funders -- government
agencies, industry partners, foundations, and international organizations. This skill
covers the core principles, sections, and strategies that make proposals compelling
regardless of the specific funding mechanism. It serves as the foundation for the
more specialized proposal skills (NSFC, provincial, industry).

## Core Principles of Winning Proposals

### 1. Tell a Compelling Story

A proposal is not a report -- it is a persuasive narrative. The best proposals
follow a story arc:
- **Hook**: Why does this problem matter right now?
- **Tension**: What is missing from current knowledge or technology?
- **Resolution**: How will your project address this gap?
- **Payoff**: What will be possible after your project succeeds?

Every section should contribute to this narrative. Disconnected sections that
read like independent documents weaken the overall proposal.

### 2. Know Your Audience

Different funders prioritize different things:
- **Government basic research funds**: Scientific novelty, theoretical contribution,
  advancing fundamental understanding.
- **Government applied research programs**: Technology readiness, practical impact,
  policy relevance, solving national needs.
- **Industry partners**: ROI, timeline, deliverables, competitive advantage.
- **Foundations**: Social impact, innovation, alignment with mission.
- **International programs**: Cross-border collaboration, capacity building,
  global challenges.

Tailor your language, emphasis, and framing to match the funder's priorities.

### 3. Be Specific, Not Vague

Vague: "We will develop an advanced algorithm to improve performance."
Specific: "We will develop a transformer-based sensor fusion algorithm that reduces
false positive rates by 30% compared to the current state-of-the-art (PointPillars)
on the KITTI benchmark, validated on our 50,000-frame adverse weather dataset."

Specificity demonstrates competence. Vagueness suggests you have not thought
the project through.

### 4. Demonstrate Feasibility

Reviewers must believe you can actually do what you propose. Demonstrate feasibility
through:
- Preliminary results that validate your approach.
- Track record of the team in related work.
- Access to necessary facilities, data, and equipment.
- A realistic timeline with appropriate milestones.
- Clear risk identification and mitigation strategies.

## Core Proposal Sections

### Section 1: Executive Summary / Abstract

**Purpose:** Give reviewers a complete picture in one page or less.

**Structure:**
- Problem statement (2-3 sentences): What gap exists?
- Proposed approach (2-3 sentences): What will you do?
- Expected outcomes (1-2 sentences): What will be achieved?
- Significance (1-2 sentences): Why does this matter?
- Budget and duration (1 sentence): How much and how long?

**Writing tip:** Write the abstract LAST, after all other sections are complete.
It should be the most polished piece of writing in the proposal.

### Section 2: Background and Significance

**Purpose:** Establish the context and justify the research need.

**Content:**
- State of the art in the field (with citations).
- Key gaps or limitations in current knowledge/technology.
- Why these gaps matter (scientific, economic, societal impact).
- How addressing these gaps connects to the funder's priorities.

**Writing strategy:**
- Use a funnel structure: start broad, then narrow to your specific gap.
- Be fair to existing work -- reviewers may be the authors of cited papers.
- Clearly articulate the gap your project will fill.
- Connect significance to the funder's mission and current priorities.

**Common mistakes:**
- Literature review that is exhaustive but does not build an argument.
- Criticizing existing work too harshly.
- Failing to explain WHY the gap matters, not just that it exists.

### Section 3: Objectives and Research Questions

**Purpose:** Define exactly what you will accomplish.

**Guidelines:**
- State 2-4 clear, measurable objectives.
- Each objective should be achievable within the proposed timeline and budget.
- Use SMART criteria: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound.
- Distinguish between overall project goals and specific objectives.

**Example:**
- Goal: Improve autonomous vehicle safety in adverse weather conditions.
- Objective 1: Develop a multi-modal sensor fusion algorithm that maintains
  detection accuracy above 90% in rain and fog conditions.
- Objective 2: Create a benchmark dataset of 100,000 annotated frames collected
  across 5 adverse weather scenarios.
- Objective 3: Validate the algorithm through 500+ km of real-world testing
  on instrumented vehicles.

### Section 4: Methodology / Technical Approach

**Purpose:** Explain HOW you will achieve your objectives.

**Structure for each research task:**
1. Task description: What will be done?
2. Rationale: Why this approach over alternatives?
3. Methods: Specific techniques, tools, and procedures.
4. Data: What data will be collected/used and how?
5. Analysis: How will results be analyzed and validated?
6. Risks and mitigation: What could go wrong and how will you handle it?

**Writing strategy:**
- Be detailed enough that a reviewer can assess feasibility.
- Reference established methods where applicable.
- For novel methods, provide theoretical justification or preliminary evidence.
- Include diagrams, flowcharts, or system architectures.
- Define success criteria for each task.

**Innovation points (创新点):**
In Chinese proposals, you must explicitly list your innovation points. Typically 3-4:
- Theoretical innovation: New models, frameworks, or theoretical contributions.
- Methodological innovation: New approaches, algorithms, or techniques.
- Application innovation: Novel applications of existing methods.
- Integration innovation: Creative combination of existing approaches.

### Section 5: Work Plan and Timeline

**Purpose:** Show that the project is well-planned and achievable.

**Elements:**
- Task breakdown structure (WBS) showing all major activities.
- Gantt chart or timeline showing parallel and sequential tasks.
- Key milestones and decision points.
- Dependencies between tasks.
- Buffer time for unexpected delays (typically 10-15% of total duration).

**Common mistake:** A work plan where everything runs in parallel with no
dependencies. This signals the plan is superficial.

### Section 6: Budget Justification

**Purpose:** Justify every expense as necessary for the project.

**Principles:**
- Every budget item should be traceable to a specific project activity.
- Justify personnel effort (FTE or person-months) for each team member.
- Equipment purchases must be justified -- explain why existing equipment
  cannot serve.
- Travel must be project-related (conferences, fieldwork, partner visits).
- Indirect costs follow funder guidelines (never negotiate these upward).

**Common budget categories:**
| Category | Typical Range | Notes |
|----------|--------------|-------|
| Personnel | 30-50% | Faculty effort, postdocs, students |
| Equipment | 10-30% | Major instruments, computing hardware |
| Materials/Consumables | 5-15% | Lab supplies, cloud computing, datasets |
| Travel | 5-10% | Conferences, fieldwork, collaboration visits |
| Services | 5-10% | Testing, characterization, subcontracts |
| Publication/IP | 3-5% | Open access fees, patent filing |
| Indirect costs | 8-20% | University overhead (funder-dependent) |

### Section 7: Team Qualifications

**Purpose:** Convince reviewers the team CAN do this work.

**For each key member:**
- Role in the project and effort commitment.
- Relevant expertise and experience (not full CV -- focused highlights).
- 3-5 key publications or accomplishments directly related to the project.
- Prior funding and project management experience.

**Team composition considerations:**
- Does the team cover all required expertise areas?
- Is there appropriate seniority mix (senior PI + junior researchers + students)?
- Are collaborators (domestic and international) included where needed?
- For Chinese proposals: are there appropriate mentorship arrangements for
  young PIs?

### Section 8: Expected Outcomes and Impact

**Purpose:** Describe the tangible results and broader significance.

**Tangible outcomes (可交付成果):**
- Publications (target journals and number)
- Patents (provisional/full, domestic/international)
- Software or datasets (with release plans)
- Prototypes or demonstrations
- Trained personnel (graduate students, postdocs)
- Standards contributions or policy recommendations

**Impact categories:**
- Scientific impact: Advancing knowledge in the field.
- Technological impact: Enabling new capabilities.
- Economic impact: Cost savings, new products, jobs.
- Social impact: Safety, health, environment.
- Educational impact: Training the next generation.

## Storytelling in Proposals

### The Problem-Solution-Impact Framework

1. **Open with a compelling problem statement** that the reviewer can relate to.
   Use concrete examples, real-world scenarios, or statistics.

2. **Build tension** by explaining why current approaches fall short. What have
   others tried, and why is it not enough?

3. **Introduce your solution** as the natural next step. Show how your approach
   addresses the specific limitations you identified.

4. **Describe the impact** of success. Paint a picture of the future state where
   your research has made a difference.

### Connecting Sections

Use transitional sentences to connect sections:
- End of Background: "These limitations motivate the proposed research, which
  addresses X by introducing Y."
- End of Objectives: "To achieve these objectives, we propose the following
  technical approach."
- End of Methodology: "The work plan below organizes these tasks into a coherent
  2-year research program."

### Visual Storytelling

- Use a single overview figure that captures the entire project conceptually.
- Place it early (page 1 or 2) so reviewers immediately grasp your vision.
- Use color coding consistently across figures.
- Annotate figures to highlight innovation points.

## The 8-Component Proposal Framework

This framework, widely adopted in competitive proposal writing, organizes a
research proposal into eight essential components. Each component serves a
specific function in building the reviewer's confidence.

### Component 1: Introduction — Set the Stage
- Introduce the topic clearly and concisely.
- Establish motivation: why does this research matter right now?
- Provide a brief preview of your proposed approach (1-2 sentences).
- The introduction should make the reviewer want to keep reading.

### Component 2: Scope — Be Very Specific
- Define the boundaries of your project precisely.
- State rough research questions that guide the investigation.
- **Principle: "Less is more."** A tightly scoped proposal is more convincing
  than an ambitious one that tries to cover too much ground.
- Avoid vague scope statements like "explore various aspects of X."
- Instead: "We will investigate the specific effect of Y on Z under conditions W."

### Component 3: Background — Demonstrate Your Qualifications
- Highlight your relevant research experience.
- Emphasize transferable skills: team management, experimental design,
  data visualization, and scientific writing.
- Show that you have the skills to execute the proposed work, not just
  conceive of it.
- Include evidence of collaboration and interdisciplinary capability.

### Component 4: Theme — Find ONE Overarching Theme
- **This is the most critical component for coherence.**
- Every proposal should have ONE overarching theme that ties all elements together.
- The theme is the "big idea" or "central thesis" that unifies your objectives,
  methodology, and expected outcomes.
- Ask yourself: "If a reviewer remembers only one thing about my proposal,
  what should it be?" That is your theme.
- All research questions, tasks, and innovations should connect back to this theme.
- A proposal without a clear theme reads as a collection of disconnected tasks.

### Component 5: Contributions — Focus on YOUR Work
- Clearly articulate what YOU will specifically contribute.
- Distinguish your contributions from what already exists in the literature.
- Avoid generic contribution statements ("advance the field").
- Instead: "We will provide the first comprehensive dataset of X under Y conditions,
  enabling future research on Z."
- Each contribution should be testable and verifiable.

### Component 6: Methodologies — Problem + Data + Methods + Experiments
- Structure each methodology section around four elements:
  1. **Problem definition**: What specific question does this task address?
  2. **Data collection**: What data will be gathered and how?
  3. **Methods**: What analytical or computational methods will be applied?
  4. **Experiments**: How will results be validated and evaluated?
- Provide enough detail for a reviewer to assess feasibility.
- Reference established methods and justify novel ones.

### Component 7: Timeline and Project Management
- Provide rough time estimates for each major task.
- Mention known constraints (equipment access, data availability, student availability).
- Show that you have thought about dependencies between tasks.
- Include buffer time (10-15%) for unexpected delays.
- A Gantt chart or milestone table is preferred over narrative descriptions.

### Component 8: Future Goals — Express Your Vision
- Articulate what you want to achieve through this research experience.
- Connect the proposed work to your longer-term research agenda.
- Show how the outcomes will enable future projects or directions.
- For student proposals: express what skills and knowledge you want to gain.
- For faculty proposals: show how this builds your research program.

### Applying the Framework

When writing a proposal, start by defining your **ONE overarching theme**
(Component 4) and your **specific contributions** (Component 5). These two
components anchor the entire document. Then build the other components around
them, ensuring every section connects back to the theme.

**Self-test:** After drafting, read only the first sentence of each section.
If those sentences alone tell a coherent story, your proposal has good structure.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Proposal by accumulation**: Listing many unconnected tasks instead of telling
   a coherent story.
2. **Overselling**: Making claims that exceed what the proposed work can deliver.
3. **Underselling**: Being so cautious that the proposal sounds unambitious.
4. **Method fetishism**: Focusing on the method without explaining why it matters.
5. **Missing the "so what"**: Describing what you will do without explaining why
   anyone should care.
6. **Copy-paste from prior proposals**: Reviewers can tell when text is recycled
   and no longer fresh.
7. **Ignoring formatting guidelines**: Exceeding page limits, using wrong fonts,
   or missing required sections.
8. **No preliminary data**: For competitive funds, you need evidence you can
   execute the proposed work.
9. **Unrealistic timeline**: Promising too much in too little time.
10. **Budget-scope mismatch**: Proposing work that would cost 5x the requested budget.

## Review Criteria Across Funders

| Criterion | NSFC Weight | Provincial Weight | Industry Weight | Foundation Weight |
|-----------|------------|-------------------|----------------|-------------------|
| Innovation/Novelty | Very High | High | Medium | High |
| Feasibility | High | High | Very High | Medium |
| Team Qualifications | High | Medium | Very High | Medium |
| Methodology Rigor | Very High | High | Medium | Medium |
| Practical Impact | Low-Medium | Very High | Very High | High |
| Budget Justification | Medium | Medium | High | Medium |
| Prior Results | High | Medium | Very High | Low |
| Alignment with Priorities | Medium | Very High | High | Very High |

## Pre-Submission Checklist

- [ ] All required sections are complete and within page limits.
- [ ] Abstract accurately summarizes the entire proposal.
- [ ] Objectives are specific, measurable, and achievable.
- [ ] Methodology matches objectives -- every objective has corresponding tasks.
- [ ] Budget matches methodology -- every task has adequate funding.
- [ ] Timeline is realistic with appropriate milestones.
- [ ] Team has all required expertise for the proposed work.
- [ ] References are complete and properly formatted.
- [ ] Required appendices and attachments are included.
- [ ] At least one colleague has reviewed the proposal.
- [ ] Formatting follows the funder's guidelines exactly.
- [ ] All co-PIs have reviewed and approved the submission.

## Prompt Templates for Claude

### Draft a Research Proposal from Scratch

```
Help me write a research proposal with the following details:

Funding body: [FUNDER NAME AND PROGRAM]
Research topic: [TOPIC]
Research field: [FIELD]
Duration: [YEARS]
Budget: [AMOUNT]

Key information:
- Problem to address: [DESCRIPTION]
- Proposed approach: [HIGH-LEVEL METHODOLOGY]
- Preliminary results: [WHAT EXISTS ALREADY]
- Team: [KEY MEMBERS AND EXPERTISE]
- Expected outcomes: [PUBLICATIONS, PATENTS, PROTOTYPES, ETC.]

Write the following sections:
1. Executive summary / Abstract
2. Background and significance (with placeholder references)
3. Research objectives (3-4 SMART objectives)
4. Methodology (task breakdown with rationale)
5. Work plan and timeline
6. Expected outcomes and impact
7. Innovation points (3-4)

Tailor the language and emphasis to [FUNDER TYPE: basic research / applied / industry].
```

### Strengthen a Weak Proposal Section

```
Review and strengthen the following proposal section:

Section type: [background / methodology / budget justification / etc.]
Funder: [FUNDER NAME]
Current text:
[PASTE SECTION]

Please:
1. Identify weaknesses (vague language, missing justification, logical gaps)
2. Suggest specific improvements
3. Rewrite the section incorporating these improvements
4. Ensure alignment with [FUNDER]'s review criteria
```

### Generate Innovation Points

```
Based on the following research description, identify and articulate 3-4 clear
innovation points (创新点) suitable for a Chinese research proposal:

Research description:
[PASTE DESCRIPTION]

Existing approaches:
[DESCRIBE WHAT OTHERS DO]

For each innovation point:
1. State the innovation concisely (1 sentence)
2. Explain what is new compared to existing work (2-3 sentences)
3. Describe the expected impact of this innovation (1-2 sentences)
```

### Create a Pre-Submission Review Checklist

```
Based on the following proposal and funder requirements, create a customized
pre-submission checklist:

Funder: [FUNDER NAME]
Proposal type: [PROGRAM]
Page limit: [PAGES]
Required sections: [LIST]
Special requirements: [ANY UNIQUE REQUIREMENTS]

Generate a detailed checklist covering:
1. Content completeness for each required section
2. Formatting compliance
3. Budget compliance with funder rules
4. Required attachments and appendices
5. Common pitfalls specific to this funder
```
