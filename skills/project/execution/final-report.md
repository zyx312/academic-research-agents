# Research Project Final Report Guide

## Purpose

This skill provides a comprehensive framework for writing final reports
(结题报告) for completed funded research projects. It covers report structure,
outcomes documentation, impact articulation, budget summary, lessons learned,
and sustainability planning. The goal is to demonstrate that project objectives
were met and that the investment produced meaningful outcomes.

---

## 1. Report Structure

### Standard Final Report Outline

```
1. Executive Summary (1-2 pages)
   - Project title, PI, funding period, total funding
   - Brief statement of objectives
   - Summary of key achievements
   - Most significant outcomes (2-3 highlights)

2. Project Objectives and Scope
   2.1 Original objectives (from proposal)
   2.2 Approved modifications (if any)
   2.3 Final scope of work completed

3. Research Activities and Methodology
   3.1 Overall research approach
   3.2 Detailed description of methods used
   3.3 Experimental setups and protocols
   3.4 Data collection and analysis procedures

4. Results and Findings
   4.1 Key research findings (organized by objective)
   4.2 Quantitative results with supporting data
   4.3 Qualitative findings and observations
   4.4 Negative results and lessons learned
   4.5 Comparison with original hypotheses

5. Outcomes and Deliverables
   5.1 Publications (complete list with citations)
   5.2 Patents and intellectual property
   5.3 Software, tools, and datasets released
   5.4 Prototypes and demonstrations
   5.5 Standards contributions

6. Impact Assessment
   6.1 Scientific impact (citations, follow-on research)
   6.2 Technological impact (tools, methods adopted)
   6.3 Educational impact (students trained, courses developed)
   6.4 Societal impact (policy influence, public benefit)
   6.5 Economic impact (industry adoption, spin-offs)

7. Personnel and Training
   7.1 Team members and roles
   7.2 PhD dissertations completed
   7.3 Master's theses completed
   7.4 Undergraduate projects supervised
   7.5 Postdoc development
   7.6 Skills and capacity built

8. Financial Summary
   8.1 Final budget vs actual expenditure
   8.2 Category-by-category accounting
   8.3 Cost-effectiveness assessment
   8.4 Unexpended funds (if any) and explanation

9. Collaboration and Partnerships
   9.1 Academic collaborations
   9.2 Industry partnerships
   9.3 International collaborations
   9.4 Community engagement

10. Sustainability and Future Directions
    10.1 How results will be maintained/used going forward
    10.2 Follow-on funding secured or planned
    10.3 Research agenda emerging from this project
    10.4 Open problems identified

11. Appendices
    11.1 Complete publication list
    11.2 Student thesis abstracts
    11.3 Patent documents
    11.4 Supplementary data and figures
    11.5 Letters of support or collaboration confirmation
```

## 2. Objectives Recap and Assessment

### Objectives Completion Matrix

| # | Original Objective | Status | Evidence | Notes |
|---|-------------------|--------|----------|-------|
| O1 | Develop algorithm for X | Fully achieved | Publication [P1], software release | Performance exceeds target |
| O2 | Collect dataset of Y | Fully achieved | Dataset on Zenodo (DOI: xxx) | 15,000 samples vs 10,000 planned |
| O3 | Demonstrate Z in real conditions | Partially achieved | Demo video, conference paper [P3] | Limited to simulation; field test deferred |
| O4 | Establish benchmark for W | Exceeded | Benchmark adopted by 3 research groups | Now standard in the community |

### Handling Partially Achieved Objectives

When an objective was not fully met, explain:

1. **What was achieved** and why it is still valuable
2. **What prevented full completion** (specific reasons)
3. **What the remaining gap is** and how it could be addressed
4. **How partial results contribute** to the field
5. **Follow-on plans** to complete the work

## 3. Results Documentation

### Organizing Results by Objective

Each original objective should map to a results section:

```
Objective 1: [State the objective]

Approach: [Brief description of method used]

Results:
  - Quantitative finding 1: [metric = value, with confidence interval]
  - Quantitative finding 2: [comparison with baseline/state-of-art]
  - Key figure/table reference: [Figure X, Table Y]

Significance:
  - Why this matters for the field
  - How it advances understanding
  - Practical implications

Publications arising: [List related papers]
```

### Presenting Negative Results

Negative results are valuable and should be included:

- Frame as "what we learned" rather than "what failed"
- Explain why the expected result did not occur
- Describe how this finding redirected the research
- Note whether the negative result has been published
- Highlight how future researchers can benefit from this knowledge

### Data and Reproducibility

Document how results can be reproduced:

- Where data is stored and how to access it
- Software versions and computational environment
- Experimental protocols and parameter settings
- Statistical tests used and significance levels

## 4. Highlighting Impact Beyond Publications

### Impact Categories and Evidence

| Impact Type | Examples | Evidence to Provide |
|------------|---------|---------------------|
| Scientific | New theory, method, dataset | Citations, adoption by other groups |
| Technological | Tool, system, prototype | Downloads, deployments, licenses |
| Educational | Trained researchers, curriculum | Degrees awarded, courses taught |
| Policy | Standards, regulations | Contributions to standards bodies |
| Societal | Public safety, environment | Deployments, media coverage |
| Economic | Industry adoption, startups | Partnerships, licensing revenue |

### Writing Impact Statements

Structure each impact claim as:

```
[What was produced] + [Who benefits] + [Evidence of adoption/impact]

Example: "The open-source detection toolkit developed in this project
has been downloaded over 2,000 times from GitHub and is now used by
three autonomous driving companies in their development pipelines,
as confirmed by their published reports."
```

### Quantifying Impact

Whenever possible, use numbers:
- Number of citations to project publications
- Software download or usage statistics
- Students who graduated and their current positions
- Industry partnerships established
- Follow-on grants secured (with amounts)
- Standards or policy documents influenced

|----------|----------|-------|----------|-----------|-------------|

| Equipment | 150,000 | 148,500 | -1,500 | -1.0% | On budget |
| Travel | 60,000 | 52,000 | -8,000 | -13.3% | One conference virtual |
| Materials | 40,000 | 43,000 | +3,000 | +7.5% | Additional sensors needed |
| Indirect | 50,000 | 50,000 | 0 | 0% | Fixed rate |
| **Total** | **600,000** | **583,500** | **-16,500** | **-2.8%** | |

### Budget Narrative Requirements

- Explain any category with more than 10% variance
- Document any approved re-budgeting actions
- Note if unspent funds need to be returned
- Describe cost-effectiveness: what was achieved per unit of funding
- If applicable, compare budget efficiency with similar projects

## 6. Lessons Learned Section

### Framework for Lessons Learned

Organize by category:

```
Research Methodology:
  - What worked well: [specific methods or approaches]
  - What would be done differently: [improvements identified]
  - Unexpected challenges: [and how they were resolved]

Project Management:
  - Timeline adherence: [what caused delays, what helped stay on track]
  - Team coordination: [effective practices and challenges]
  - Resource utilization: [efficient uses and waste areas]

Collaboration:
  - What strengthened partnerships: [specific practices]
  - Communication challenges: [and solutions found]
  - Data sharing: [lessons about data exchange protocols]

Technical:
  - Tool and platform choices: [what worked, what did not]
  - Scalability issues: [discovered during the project]
  - Reproducibility: [challenges in replicating results]
```

## 7. Sustainability Plan

### Ensuring Project Outcomes Persist

Address each category of output:

| Output | Sustainability Action | Responsible Party | Timeline |
|--------|----------------------|-------------------|----------|
| Software tools | Open-source release, documentation | PI + lab | 6 months post-project |
| Datasets | Archive on Zenodo/Figshare with DOI | Data manager | Before project end |
| Publications | Open access versions on repository | Authors | Ongoing |
| Student expertise | Alumni network, mentoring continuation | PI | Ongoing |
| Industry relationships | Follow-on contracts, advisory board | PI | 1 year post-project |
| Standards contributions | Continue committee participation | PI | Ongoing |

### Follow-on Funding Strategy

- Identify 2-3 follow-on grant opportunities
- Describe how this project's results create new fundable questions
- Note any letters of intent or preliminary discussions with funders
- Reference any spin-off projects already initiated

---

## 8. Quality Checklist

Before submitting the final report:

- [ ] All original objectives are addressed with status assessment
- [ ] Results are organized by objective with quantitative evidence
- [ ] Complete publication list with full citations is included
- [ ] Student training outcomes (degrees, skills) are documented
- [ ] Budget summary is complete with variance explanations
- [ ] Impact beyond publications is articulated with evidence
- [ ] Lessons learned section provides genuine insights
- [ ] Sustainability plan addresses all major outputs
- [ ] Appendices include all required supporting materials
- [ ] Executive summary captures the most compelling achievements
- [ ] The report is proofread and formatted per funder requirements
- [ ] All co-PIs and key personnel have reviewed the report

---

## 9. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Listing activities instead of outcomes:** Focus on what was achieved, not
   just what was done
2. **Ignoring objectives that were not met:** Address all objectives honestly
3. **Weak impact section:** Go beyond listing papers; show real-world effect
4. **Incomplete budget reporting:** Every category needs accounting
5. **No future direction:** Show that the project generated new questions
6. **Missing student outcomes:** Funders care about training impact
7. **Boilerplate lessons learned:** Provide specific, actionable insights
8. **Forgetting acknowledgments:** Credit the funder properly and consistently

```
1. Clear objective-by-objective assessment
```


### Executive Summary Writer

```
Write an executive summary for a research project final report.

Project: [TITLE]
Duration: [YEARS]
Funding: [AMOUNT AND SOURCE]
Objectives: [LIST]
Key achievements: [LIST TOP 5]
Most significant finding: [DESCRIBE]
Impact highlights: [2-3 BULLET POINTS]
Publications count: [N JOURNALS, M CONFERENCES]
Students graduated: [N PhD, M MS]

Generate a 300-500 word executive summary that:
1. Opens with the problem addressed and its significance
2. States the objectives concisely
3. Highlights the 3 most important achievements
4. Quantifies the project's output and impact
5. Ends with the significance of the work for the field
6. Is suitable for a non-specialist reader at the funding agency
```

### Lessons Learned Extractor

```
Help me write a substantive lessons learned section for a final report.

Project challenges and how they were handled:
[DESCRIBE 5-10 CHALLENGES OR NOTABLE EVENTS]

What went well:
[DESCRIBE SUCCESSFUL APPROACHES AND PRACTICES]

What could be improved:
[DESCRIBE AREAS FOR IMPROVEMENT]

Generate a lessons learned section that:
1. Covers research methodology, project management, collaboration, and technical areas
2. Provides specific rather than generic insights
3. Offers actionable recommendations for similar future projects
4. Maintains a constructive and forward-looking tone
5. Is approximately 400-600 words
```
