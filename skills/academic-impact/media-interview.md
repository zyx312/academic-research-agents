# Media Interview Preparation Skill

## Overview

This skill prepares academics for effective media engagement, including interviews with print journalists, TV/radio broadcasters, podcasters, and online media. It covers key message development, soundbite crafting, bridging techniques, and handling difficult questions across both Chinese and international media contexts.

## Why Media Engagement Matters for Academics

- **Research impact**: Public communication amplifies the societal impact of your work
- **Funding justification**: Demonstrating public engagement supports grant renewals
- **Institutional value**: Media visibility raises your university's profile
- **Policy influence**: Clear communication can shape policy discussions
- **Career advancement**: Media engagement is increasingly valued in evaluations (社会服务)
- **Public trust**: Direct communication builds trust in science and research

## Preparing Key Messages

### The Message Triangle

Every media interaction should be organized around three key messages:

1. **The Finding**: What did you discover or what is your expert opinion?
2. **The Significance**: Why does this matter to the public?
3. **The Next Step**: What should happen next (further research, policy changes, public action)?

### Message Development Process

1. **Identify the core message**: Distill your expertise into one sentence a non-expert can understand and repeat
2. **Support with evidence**: Prepare 2-3 specific examples, data points, or analogies for each key message
3. **Anticipate the headline**: What do you want the article title to be? Work backward from there
4. **Test with non-experts**: Ask a friend or family member to listen and repeat your main point

### Key Message Worksheet

For each interview, prepare:
- **One-sentence finding**: "[Our research shows / The evidence suggests] that [X], which means [Y] for [audience]"
- **Supporting fact 1**: A specific number, statistic, or example
- **Supporting fact 2**: A comparison or analogy that makes the finding tangible
- **Supporting fact 3**: A real-world application or implication
- **The "so what"**: Why should a non-specialist care?

## Soundbite Creation

### What Makes a Good Soundbite
- **Length**: 10-20 seconds when spoken (15-30 words)
- **Structure**: Complete thought that stands alone without context
- **Language**: Conversational, jargon-free, concrete
- **Memorability**: Uses vivid imagery, analogy, or contrast

### Soundbite Formulas

1. **Analogy**: "Think of [complex concept] like [familiar thing]. Just as [familiar process], our research shows [finding]."
2. **Contrast**: "While most people assume [common belief], what we actually found is [surprising result]."
3. **Scale**: "[Finding] affects [large number] of people every year. That's equivalent to [vivid comparison]."
4. **If-then**: "If we don't address [problem], we can expect [consequence] within [timeframe]."
5. **Three-part list**: "This matters for three reasons: [A], [B], and [C]."
6. **Personal stake**: "This means that [everyday person/scenario] could [benefit/risk]."

### Soundbite Do's and Don'ts

Do:
- Use active voice and present tense
- Include specific numbers and concrete examples
- Speak in terms of people, not abstractions
- Practice until delivery feels natural, not scripted

Don't:
- Use jargon, acronyms, or technical terms without explanation
- Hedge excessively ("We think it might possibly suggest...")
- Use academic qualifiers that weaken the message
- Start with "That's a great question" (wastes airtime)

## Bridging Techniques

Bridging allows you to move from a question you don't want to answer to a message you do want to deliver.

### Bridge Phrases

- "That's an important point, and what's even more important is..."
- "I understand the concern, but let me put this in context..."
- "While that's part of the picture, the key issue is..."
- "Before we go further, let me emphasize that..."
- "What the research actually shows is..."
- "The real question here is..."
- "Let me take a step back and explain why..."

### The ABC Method

- **A**cknowledge the question (briefly, don't repeat negative framings)
- **B**ridge to your message (use a bridge phrase)
- **C**ommunicate your key point (deliver your soundbite)

Example:
- Reporter: "Isn't this technology dangerous?"
- Response: "Safety is absolutely central to our work. [bridge] That's exactly why our research focuses on [key message]. We've developed methods that [evidence], which actually makes the technology safer than current alternatives."

## Handling Difficult Questions

### Types of Difficult Questions and Responses

**The Speculative Question**: "What if this technology fails catastrophically?"
- Strategy: Acknowledge the concern, redirect to evidence and safeguards
- "We take failure scenarios very seriously. That's why our research includes [specific safety measures]. What the data shows is..."

**The Leading Question**: "Don't you think your research is irresponsible?"
- Strategy: Reject the premise politely, reframe
- "I'd frame it differently. Our research is specifically designed to address [problem], and the results show [positive outcome]."

**The Off-Topic Question**: "What do you think about [unrelated controversy]?"
- Strategy: Stay in your lane
- "That's outside my area of expertise. What I can speak to is [your topic], where..."

**The "Can You Guarantee?" Question**: "Can you guarantee this is safe?"
- Strategy: Be honest about uncertainty without creating alarm
- "In science, we work with evidence and probability. What the evidence strongly supports is..."

**The Hypothetical**: "What would happen if [unlikely extreme scenario]?"
- Strategy: Ground the conversation in reality
- "While hypotheticals are interesting to consider, let me tell you what we actually know based on our data..."

**The Gotcha Question**: Designed to create a controversial quote
- Strategy: Pause, think, respond carefully
- Never repeat a negative framing. Restate in your own positive terms

### Universal Rules for Difficult Questions

1. **Pause before answering**: 2-3 seconds of silence is fine and shows thoughtfulness
2. **Never say "No comment"**: Instead, explain why you cannot answer (ongoing peer review, not your area, confidentiality)
3. **Never go "off the record"**: Assume everything can be quoted
4. **Correct errors immediately**: If you misspeak, say "Let me rephrase that..."
5. **Stay calm**: Emotional responses make bad soundbites
6. **It is acceptable to say "I don't know"**: Follow with "But what I do know is..."

## Different Media Types

### Print / Online Journalism
- **Pace**: Slower, allows for nuance and detail
- **Length**: Longer quotes possible (2-3 sentences)
- **Control**: Reporter selects quotes; you cannot control what is used
- **Tips**: Ask to review quotes (not the whole article) before publication; follow up with additional context by email

### Television
- **Pace**: Very fast, soundbites of 10-15 seconds
- **Appearance**: Dress professionally, avoid busy patterns, look at the interviewer (not the camera, unless instructed otherwise)
- **Tips**: Sit forward slightly, use hand gestures moderately, speak in complete sentences that can stand alone as clips
- **Live vs. recorded**: Live allows no editing but more control; recorded allows re-takes but the final cut is the editor's choice

### Radio / Podcast
- **Pace**: Moderate, conversational
- **Length**: More room for explanation than TV, less than print
- **Tips**: Voice energy matters more than visual presence; smile while speaking (it affects tone); avoid paper rustling
- **Podcast advantage**: Longer format allows deeper discussion; listeners are more engaged

### Social Media / Online
- **Pace**: Instant, continuous
- **Length**: Brief (tweet-length quotes, short video clips)
- **Tips**: Provide shareable quotes and graphics; be aware that clips can be taken out of context
- **Opportunity**: You can shape the narrative through your own channels

## Building Media Relationships

### Proactive Strategies
- Register with your university press office and relevant expert databases
- Create a media-friendly profile page with your expertise areas and contact
- Write op-eds or commentary pieces for major outlets
- Respond promptly to journalist inquiries (within hours, not days)
- Share useful background information even when you are not the story
- Pitch story ideas tied to your research (connect to news cycles)

### Working with Your University Press Office
- Alert them when you have a publication with public interest
- Collaborate on press releases (you provide accuracy, they provide reach)
- Ask them to pitch stories to their media contacts
- Request media training sessions (many universities offer this)
- Coordinate timing with journal embargo policies

### Media Expert Databases
- Register on platforms where journalists find experts
- Maintain updated profiles with current research topics
- Respond quickly when contacted through these platforms
- In China: work with university publicity departments (宣传部) and science communication platforms

## Crisis Communication Basics

### When Research Becomes Controversial

1. **Prepare in advance**: Have a crisis message ready for foreseeable controversies
2. **Respond quickly**: Silence is interpreted as guilt or indifference
3. **Lead with facts**: State what is known, acknowledge what is uncertain
4. **Show empathy**: If people are affected, acknowledge their concerns first
5. **Stay consistent**: All team members should deliver the same key messages
6. **Correct misinformation**: Provide facts calmly, without attacking the source
7. **Know when to stop**: Avoid feeding a controversy with excessive responses

### Crisis Message Template
"We are aware of [situation]. Our priority is [stakeholder well-being / scientific accuracy]. Here is what we know: [facts]. We are [specific action being taken]. We will provide updates as more information becomes available."

## Do's and Don'ts Summary

### Do:
- Prepare 3 key messages before any interview
- Speak in clear, jargon-free language
- Use concrete examples and vivid analogies
- Stay in your area of expertise
- Follow up after interviews to provide additional context
- Practice soundbites until they feel natural
- Record yourself and review for improvement

### Don't:
- Wing it without preparation
- Use jargon or assume the audience has background knowledge
- Speculate beyond the evidence
- Criticize colleagues or competitors by name
- Say anything you would not want to see as a headline
- Ignore the journalist's deadline
- Refuse all media requests (build the relationship)

## Prompt Templates

### Prepare for a Media Interview

```
I have a media interview scheduled:
- Media outlet: [name, type: print/TV/radio/podcast]
- Topic: [what they want to discuss]
- My research finding: [key result]
- Audience: [general public, industry, policy makers]
- Duration: [expected length]
- Format: [live, recorded, phone, in-studio]

Please help me prepare:
1. Three key messages using the Message Triangle framework
2. Three soundbites (10-20 seconds each) for the most important points
3. Answers to 5 likely questions the journalist will ask
4. Answers to 3 difficult or hostile questions
5. Bridge phrases for redirecting off-topic questions
6. One strong analogy that makes my finding accessible
7. Opening and closing statements
```

### Develop a Press Release Summary

```
Draft a media-friendly summary of this research:
- Paper title: [title]
- Key finding: [one sentence]
- Method: [brief description]
- Significance: [why it matters]
- Practical implications: [real-world applications]

The summary should:
1. Lead with the most newsworthy finding
2. Explain the research in language accessible to a general audience
3. Include 2-3 quotable statements from the researcher
4. Provide context (how this advances the field)
5. Suggest a compelling headline
6. Be 300-500 words total
```

### Prepare Crisis Communication

```
A potential media controversy is emerging around:
- Topic: [what happened or what might be reported]
- Facts: [what we know for certain]
- Unknowns: [what we don't yet know]
- Stakeholders affected: [who is impacted]
- Institutional position: [university stance, if any]

Help me prepare:
1. A holding statement for immediate use (3-4 sentences)
2. Three key messages for extended interviews
3. Answers to the 5 most likely challenging questions
4. Guidance on what NOT to say
5. A plan for follow-up communication
6. Talking points for internal communication with colleagues and students
```

### Train for On-Camera Presence

```
I have an upcoming TV or video interview about [topic].
This is my [first / Nth] on-camera appearance.

Please provide:
1. A checklist for physical preparation (clothing, setting, lighting)
2. Tips for managing on-camera nervousness
3. Practice exercises for delivering soundbites naturally
4. Common body language mistakes to avoid
5. Guidance on eye contact, posture, and hand gestures
6. How to handle technical difficulties (audio drops, connection issues)
```
