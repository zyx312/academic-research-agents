# Citation Tracking Skill

## Overview

This skill helps academics systematically track, analyze, and leverage citation metrics for career development. It covers traditional metrics (h-index, citation counts), alternative metrics (altmetrics), tracking platforms, alert systems, and the ethical use of metrics without gaming.

## Core Citation Metrics

### H-Index
- **Definition**: A researcher has an h-index of h if h of their papers have been cited at least h times each
- **Example**: h-index of 25 means 25 papers have each been cited 25 or more times
- **Strengths**: Balances quantity (number of papers) and quality (citation impact); robust to outliers
- **Limitations**: Cannot decrease over time; penalizes early-career researchers; field-dependent; inflated by self-citation; does not distinguish author order or contribution level

### Typical H-Index Benchmarks (field-dependent)
- New PhD graduate: 1-5
- Early career (assistant professor): 5-15
- Mid-career (associate professor): 15-30
- Senior (full professor): 25-50+
- National-level talent (杰青/长江 level): 30-60+
- Note: These ranges vary dramatically by field. A biologist's h-index of 40 may be equivalent to a mathematician's h-index of 15

### I10-Index
- **Definition**: Number of publications with at least 10 citations
- **Used by**: Google Scholar
- **Value**: Simple indicator of papers that have made some impact
- **Limitations**: Arbitrary threshold; field-dependent

### Total Citation Count
- **Definition**: Sum of all citations across all publications
- **Value**: Simple, widely understood
- **Limitations**: Inflated by one highly-cited paper; does not reflect breadth of impact

### Field-Weighted Citation Impact (FWCI)
- **Definition**: Ratio of actual citations to expected citations for the field, year, and document type
- **Value**: FWCI of 1.0 = world average; 2.0 = twice the expected citations
- **Used by**: Scopus / SciVal
- **Advantage**: Enables cross-field comparison by normalizing for disciplinary norms

### Other Metrics

- **g-index**: Top g papers have together received g-squared or more citations (rewards highly-cited papers more than h-index)
- **Citation half-life**: Median age of citations (indicates whether impact is recent or sustained)
- **Cited reference count**: Number of unique papers citing your work (vs. total citation count which includes multiple citations from the same paper)
- **Highly Cited Papers**: Papers in the top 1% of citations for their field and year (Web of Science Essential Science Indicators)

## Tracking Platforms

### Google Scholar
- **Coverage**: Broadest (journals, conferences, books, theses, preprints, patents)
- **Profile setup**: Free; claim and curate your profile at scholar.google.com
- **Metrics provided**: h-index, i10-index, total citations, citation graph over time
- **Alerts**: Email alerts for new citations to your work or new papers by keywords
- **Pros**: Most inclusive, free, easy to set up, includes Chinese-language publications
- **Cons**: May include non-peer-reviewed sources; duplicate entries common; requires manual curation

**Google Scholar maintenance tasks:**
- Merge duplicate entries
- Remove incorrectly attributed papers
- Verify author name variants are captured
- Update affiliation when changing institutions
- Review co-author network for accuracy

### Web of Science (Clarivate)
- **Coverage**: Curated journals (SCIE, SSCI, AHCI, ESCI)
- **Metrics**: h-index, total citations, Highly Cited Papers, Hot Papers
- **Profile**: Researcher profile through Web of Science
- **Pros**: Most respected for promotion and tenure evaluation; quality-controlled
- **Cons**: Narrower coverage than Google Scholar; subscription required; weak on conference proceedings (except CPCI)

### Scopus (Elsevier)
- **Coverage**: Curated journals and conference proceedings, broader than WoS
- **Metrics**: h-index, citation count, FWCI (via SciVal), document count
- **Profile**: Scopus Author ID (auto-generated, may need merging)
- **Pros**: Better conference coverage than WoS; FWCI enables field normalization
- **Cons**: Subscription required; author profiles sometimes fragmented

### Semantic Scholar
- **Coverage**: Computer science, biomedical, and expanding to other fields
- **Unique features**: AI-powered citation context analysis, influential citations, citation velocity
- **Profile**: Free, auto-generated
- **Pros**: Distinguishes influential citations from incidental ones; free; API access for data analysis
- **Cons**: Coverage gaps in some fields

### CNKI (中国知网)
- **Coverage**: Chinese-language journals, dissertations, conference papers
- **Metrics**: Citation count, download count
- **Importance**: Essential for tracking impact of Chinese-language publications
- **Used in**: Chinese university evaluations and talent program applications

### Dimensions
- **Coverage**: Broad, including grants, patents, clinical trials, policy documents
- **Unique features**: Links publications to grants and clinical trials
- **Free tier**: Basic access available without subscription

## Setting Up Alerts

### Google Scholar Alerts
1. Go to scholar.google.com
2. Search for your name or paper title
3. Click the envelope icon to create an alert
4. Set up alerts for:
   - Your own name (track new citations to your work)
   - Key collaborators or competitors
   - Specific topic keywords (track field developments)
   - Specific papers you want to monitor

### Web of Science / Scopus Alerts
- Set up citation alerts for individual papers
- Create saved searches with email notifications
- Use journal alert features for key publications in your field

### Research Rabbit / Connected Papers
- Visual tools for tracking citation networks
- Discover related papers through citation graphs
- Free alternatives to manual citation tracking

### Advanced Citation Analysis Tools

**Publish or Perish (harzing.com/resources/publish-or-perish)**
- Free desktop application for citation analysis.
- Queries Google Scholar, Scopus, Web of Science, Crossref, and Microsoft Academic.
- Computes comprehensive metrics: h-index, g-index, e-index, hI-index (individual h-index
  that accounts for co-authorship), age-weighted citation rate (AWCR), and more.
- Key uses for citation tracking:
  1. Generate detailed citation reports for specific authors or paper sets.
  2. Compare your citation profile against peers or field averages.
  3. Identify your fastest-growing papers (citation velocity analysis).
  4. Prepare quantitative evidence for promotion dossiers and talent applications.
  5. Track changes in your metrics over time by saving periodic snapshots.
- Export results to CSV, BibTeX, or clipboard for inclusion in reports.
- Run quarterly and maintain a spreadsheet of metric snapshots over time.

**Synthical (synthical.com)**
- AI-powered research discovery tool that tracks citation patterns.
- Highlights emerging trends and influential new papers in your field.
- Provides simplified summaries of cited papers, helping you quickly assess
  relevance without reading full texts.
- Useful for monitoring which of your papers are being cited and in what context.

**Connected Papers (connectedpapers.com)**
- Build visual citation graphs around any paper to see its context in the literature.
- Two key views: "Prior Work" (papers that influenced it) and "Derivative Work"
  (papers that built upon it).
- Use to track how your own influential papers spawn new research directions.
- Track field influence by building graphs around your key papers quarterly
  and noting new derivative work that appears.

### Google Scholar Alert Setup: Step-by-Step Guide

Set up these four types of alerts to comprehensively monitor your citations:

**Alert Type 1: Citations to Your Papers**
1. Go to scholar.google.com
2. Search for a specific paper title
3. Click "Cited by [N]" to see citing papers
4. Click the envelope icon (mail alert) to receive email notifications
5. Repeat for each of your key papers (at minimum, your top 10 cited papers)

**Alert Type 2: Your Name (New Citations)**
1. Go to your Google Scholar profile
2. Click "Follow" on your own profile
3. You will receive notifications when new papers cite any of your work
4. If you do not have a profile, search for your name and create an alert

**Alert Type 3: Keyword Alerts (Field Monitoring)**
1. Go to scholar.google.com
2. Enter keywords related to your research (e.g., "SOTIF autonomous driving safety")
3. Click the envelope icon on the search results page
4. Set up 3-5 keyword alerts covering your core research topics
5. These alerts track new publications in your field, not just citations to your work

**Alert Type 4: Competitor/Collaborator Monitoring**
1. Search for key researchers in your field by name
2. Follow their Google Scholar profiles
3. Set up alerts for their new publications
4. This helps you stay current with the state of the art and anticipate
   how your work will be positioned relative to peers

**Recommended alert management:**
- Review alerts weekly (batch process, do not check daily)
- Maintain a "citation inbox" folder in your email for tracking
- Monthly: review new citations for papers that should be read in full
- Quarterly: update your citation metrics record using Publish or Perish

## Understanding Citation Patterns

### Normal Citation Trajectory
- Most papers receive the majority of citations within 3-5 years of publication
- Peak citation year is typically 2-3 years post-publication
- "Sleeping beauties" are rare papers that receive delayed recognition
- Review papers and methodological papers tend to have longer citation half-lives

### What Drives Citations
- Journal prestige and visibility
- Open access availability
- Title clarity and keyword richness
- Self-promotion through social media and presentations
- Quality and novelty of the research itself
- Timeliness relative to field trends
- Collaboration networks (more co-authors = more citation networks)

### Red Flags in Citation Patterns
- Sudden spikes may indicate citation rings or manipulation
- Very high self-citation rates (>25%) raise ethical concerns
- Citation counts far above field norms warrant scrutiny
- "Citation cartels" between collaborating groups

## Self-Citation Ethics

### Acceptable Self-Citation
- Citing your own prior work when it is genuinely relevant
- Building on your previous findings (demonstrating a research program)
- Providing methodological context from your earlier papers
- Typical healthy self-citation rate: 5-15% of total citations

### Problematic Self-Citation
- Citing your own work when equally relevant alternatives exist from other groups
- Excessive self-citation to inflate metrics
- Arranging mutual citation with collaborators for metric purposes
- Including unnecessary references to your own work in reviews or editorials

### Guidelines
- Let relevance drive citation decisions, not metric goals
- Be prepared to justify every self-citation if asked
- Monitor your self-citation rate through Scopus (which calculates it automatically)
- Some talent programs and evaluations now discount or flag excessive self-citation

## Alternative Metrics (Altmetrics)

### What Altmetrics Measure
- **News mentions**: Coverage in mainstream and scientific media
- **Social media**: Shares, likes, and comments on Twitter/X, Facebook, LinkedIn, Weibo
- **Policy documents**: Citations in government or institutional policy papers
- **Wikipedia**: References in Wikipedia articles
- **Blog posts**: Coverage in academic and professional blogs
- **Mendeley readers**: Saves in reference manager (indicates academic interest)
- **Downloads**: Full-text download counts from publisher or repository

### Altmetric Attention Score (Altmetric.com)
- Weighted aggregate of online attention across sources
- Displayed as a colorful donut badge
- News and blog coverage weighted higher than social media
- Free bookmarklet available for checking individual papers
- Does not measure quality, only attention

### PlumX Metrics (Elsevier/Plum Analytics)
- Five categories: Usage, Captures, Mentions, Social Media, Citations
- Integrated into Scopus
- Broader scope than Altmetric.com
- Useful for demonstrating broader impact in grant reports

### Using Altmetrics Effectively
- Complement (do not replace) traditional citation metrics
- Useful for demonstrating societal impact in grant applications
- Track media engagement and public interest in your work
- Early indicator of which papers are gaining attention
- Include in annual reports and impact narratives
- Particularly useful for applied research with policy implications

## Using Metrics for Career Development

### For Promotion and Tenure
- Present metrics in context (field averages, peer comparisons)
- Use multiple metrics, not just h-index
- Include narrative explanation of impact (not just numbers)
- Highlight highly-cited individual papers and their influence
- Address any anomalies (e.g., field differences, career breaks)

### For Grant Applications
- Demonstrate track record with citation evidence
- Show impact of prior funded work
- Use FWCI or field-normalized metrics for interdisciplinary work
- Include altmetrics to show broader impact

### For Talent Program Applications (人才计划)
- Select representative works (代表性成果) that show highest impact
- Provide field context for citation numbers
- Include international recognition indicators
- Show citation trajectory (growth over time)
- Compare to field benchmarks honestly

### What NOT to Do
- Do not game metrics through citation manipulation
- Do not compare across fields without normalization
- Do not judge quality solely by citation count
- Do not obsess over small changes in h-index
- Do not use metrics as the only measure of contribution
- Do not disparage colleagues based on metric comparisons

## Prompt Templates

### Generate a Citation Report

```
Help me create a citation metrics report:
- My Google Scholar profile: [URL or key stats]
- My field: [discipline]
- Career stage: [years since PhD, current title]
- Purpose: [annual review, promotion dossier, grant application, talent program]

Please generate:
1. Summary table of key metrics (h-index, i10-index, total citations, recent 5-year citations)
2. Analysis of citation trajectory (growth pattern over time)
3. Identification of top-cited papers and their impact
4. Field context (how do my metrics compare to typical benchmarks?)
5. Recommendations for improving citation visibility
6. Any concerns to be aware of (self-citation rate, anomalies)
```

### Set Up a Citation Tracking System

```
Help me design a systematic approach to tracking my citations:
- Current platforms I use: [Google Scholar, WoS, Scopus, etc.]
- Number of publications: [count]
- Primary fields: [disciplines]
- Career goals: [what I need metrics for]

Please recommend:
1. Which platforms to set up profiles on and why
2. Step-by-step alert configuration for each platform
3. A quarterly review checklist for citation maintenance
4. How to handle discrepancies between platforms
5. Tools for visualizing citation networks and trends
6. A template for recording and tracking metrics over time
```

### Prepare a Research Impact Narrative

```
I need to write a research impact statement for [promotion/grant/award]:
- My key metrics: [h-index, total citations, key paper citations]
- Representative papers: [list top 3-5 papers with citation counts]
- Field: [discipline]
- Altmetric highlights: [media coverage, policy citations, etc.]

Please draft a 500-word research impact narrative that:
1. Presents metrics in appropriate field context
2. Tells the story behind the numbers (what problems were solved)
3. Highlights individual paper impacts with specific examples
4. Includes broader impact indicators (altmetrics, policy influence)
5. Demonstrates a positive trajectory
6. Avoids over-reliance on any single metric
7. Addresses any potential weaknesses proactively
```

### Analyze Citation Patterns for a Paper

```
Help me analyze the citation impact of this paper:
- Paper title: [title]
- Published: [year, journal]
- Current citation count: [number across platforms]
- Field: [discipline]
- Self-citations: [number, if known]

Please analyze:
1. Is this citation count above, at, or below field average for its age?
2. What is the citation trajectory (growing, peaked, declining)?
3. What types of papers are citing it (reviews, empirical, methods)?
4. Is the citation pattern healthy or does it show concerning patterns?
5. Suggestions for increasing the paper's visibility and impact
6. How to position this paper in a promotion or award application
```
