# Research Progress Tracking Guide

## Purpose

This skill provides templates, frameworks, and strategies for systematically
tracking graduate student research progress. It covers meeting formats,
milestone monitoring, early warning systems, individual development plans,
and publication pipeline management to ensure timely degree completion.

---

## 3. Early Warning Signs and Interventions

### Academic Warning Signs

| Warning Sign | Severity | Intervention |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| Missed 2+ weekly meetings | Medium | Direct conversation about reasons |
| No progress for 3+ weeks | High | Emergency meeting; identify root cause |
| Milestone delayed > 1 month | Medium | Reassess timeline; adjust scope |
| Milestone delayed > 3 months | High | Formal progress review with committee |
| Failing or withdrawing from courses | High | Academic support referral; load reduction |
| Cannot articulate research question | Medium | Structured reading + writing exercise |
| Avoiding writing entirely | Medium | Set micro-deadlines; require weekly pages |
| No publication attempts by Year 3 (PhD) | High | Intensive paper-writing plan |

### Personal Warning Signs

| Warning Sign | Severity | Intervention |
|-------------|----------|-------------|
| Social withdrawal from lab | Medium | Check in privately; invite to social events |
| Expressed anxiety about progress | Medium | Normalize struggles; adjust expectations |
| Significant life events | Variable | Offer flexibility; discuss leave if needed |
| Burnout indicators (cynicism, exhaustion) | High | Reduce workload; encourage time off |
| Financial stress | High | Identify funding options; RA/TA positions |
| Advisor-student relationship tension | High | Mediated conversation; involve department |

### Intervention Escalation Protocol

```
Level 1: Informal (advisor only)
  - Candid 1-on-1 conversation
  - Adjust immediate expectations
  - Set short-term recovery goals (2-4 weeks)

Level 2: Structured (advisor + student agreement)
  - Written progress improvement plan
  - More frequent meetings (2x/week)
  - Clear deliverables with weekly deadlines
  - 4-8 week review period

Level 3: Formal (advisor + committee/department)
  - Committee meeting to assess progress
  - Formal letter outlining expectations
  - Consider scope reduction or degree change
  - Timeline ultimatum with consequences

Level 4: Administrative (department level)
  - Probationary status
  - Advisor change consideration
  - Leave of absence option
  - Program withdrawal discussion
```

## 4. Group Meeting Formats

### Format A: Round-Robin Updates (30-60 min)

Best for: Small groups (3-6 students), weekly frequency

```
Structure:
  - Each student: 5-minute update + 3-minute Q&A
  - Focus: what did you do, what will you do, what is blocking you
  - Advisor provides brief feedback after each update
  - End with lab announcements (5 min)
```

### Format B: Deep Dive Rotation (60-90 min)

Best for: Larger groups (6-12 students), weekly or bi-weekly

```
Structure:
  - One student presents in depth (30-40 min)
  - Formal slides with problem, method, results
  - Full group discussion and feedback (20-30 min)
  - Brief updates from 2-3 others (10-15 min)
  - Rotation schedule ensures each student presents every 4-6 weeks
```

### Format C: Paper Reading Club + Updates (60-90 min)

Best for: Groups wanting to stay current with literature

```
Structure:
  - One student presents a recent paper (20 min)
  - Group discussion of paper's relevance to lab research (15 min)
  - Round-robin progress updates (30 min)
  - Builds reading culture and critical analysis skills
```

### Format D: Working Session (90-120 min)

Best for: Collaborative projects, deadline pushes

```
Structure:
  - Brief check-in (10 min)
  - Focused work time with advisor available for questions (60-90 min)
  - Students work on their own tasks but can ask questions as needed
  - Wrap-up: what was accomplished, next steps (10 min)
```

## 6. Publication Pipeline Tracking

### Pipeline Board Template

Track papers through their lifecycle using a Kanban-style board:

```
+-----------+-----------+-----------+-----------+-----------+
| Ideation  | Drafting  | Internal  | Submitted | Published |
|           |           | Review    |           |           |
+-----------+-----------+-----------+-----------+-----------+
| Paper E   | Paper D   | Paper C   | Paper B   | Paper A   |
| (concept) | (writing  | (advisor  | (Jour. X, | (Conf Y,  |
|           |  ch. 3-4) |  rev. #2) |  Jan 15)  |  Sep 23)  |
+-----------+-----------+-----------+-----------+-----------+

Paper Detail Card:
  Title: ___________________________
  Authors: _________________________
  Target venue: ____________________
  Submission deadline: ______________
  Current stage: ____________________
  Days in current stage: ____________
  Blockers: _________________________
  Next action: ______________________
  Responsible person: _______________
```

### Publication Velocity Metrics

Track these metrics for each student:

- **Time from idea to submission:** target 3-6 months
- **Review turnaround:** track days from submission to decision
- **Revision turnaround:** target 2-4 weeks for revisions
- **Acceptance rate:** track across venues to calibrate targeting
- **Papers per year:** monitor trend over time

## 7. Documentation Requirements

### What to Document and Where

| Document Type | Frequency | Storage | Purpose |
|--------------|-----------|---------|---------|
| Weekly progress notes | Weekly | Shared doc/wiki | Track tactical progress |
| Meeting minutes | Per meeting | Shared doc/wiki | Record decisions and actions |
| Monthly reports | Monthly | Lab management system | Operational oversight |
| Milestone records | Per milestone | Department system | Formal progress verification |
| IDP | Annually | Student + advisor files | Career and skill development |
| Publication records | Ongoing | Lab database/spreadsheet | Track research output |
| Research notebook | Daily/weekly | Student's own system | Experimental reproducibility |
| Code repositories | Ongoing | GitHub/GitLab | Technical reproducibility |

### Research Notebook Best Practices

Every student should maintain a research notebook (digital or physical):

1. **Date every entry**
2. **Record hypotheses before running experiments**
3. **Document all parameters and configurations**
4. **Include negative results** (what did not work and why)
5. **Link to code commits and data files**
6. **Note decisions and their rationale**
7. **Back up regularly** (weekly minimum)

## 8. Tracking Tools and Systems

### Lightweight (Small Group, 1-5 Students)

- **Google Sheets:** Milestone tracker with conditional formatting
- **Shared Google Doc:** Weekly progress log per student
- **Trello / Notion:** Kanban board for publication pipeline
- **Regular email:** Monthly summary from each student

### Medium (Research Group, 5-15 Students)

- **Notion workspace:** Centralized database with views per student
- **GitHub Projects:** For technically-oriented groups
- **Slack channel:** #progress-updates for async updates
- **Quarterly group reviews:** Formal assessment meetings

### Institutional (Department/University Level)

- **Graduate tracking systems:** Formal milestone recording
- **Annual progress reviews:** Committee-level assessment
- **University reporting tools:** For accreditation and compliance

### Early Warning Report

```
Analyze multiple progress indicators to identify at-risk students.

Student data (for each student in the group):
[PASTE: name, year, last meeting attendance, last progress submission date,
current milestone, days since last milestone completion, publication count,
advisor's concern notes]

For each student, assess:
1. Risk level: [Low / Medium / High / Critical]
2. Risk factors identified
3. Recommended intervention level (1-4, per the escalation protocol)
4. Specific actions the advisor should take this week
5. Whether external support (department, counseling) should be involved

Prioritize the report by risk level, highest first.
```

### Publication Pipeline Review

```
Review the research group's publication pipeline and provide recommendations.

Pipeline data:
[PASTE: paper title, authors, target venue, current stage, days in stage,
submission deadline, blockers]

Analyze:
1. Pipeline health: are there enough papers in early stages to sustain output?
2. Bottlenecks: which papers are stuck and why?
3. Deadline alignment: are any papers at risk of missing submission deadlines?
4. Workload distribution: is any student overloaded or underloaded?
5. Venue strategy: are target venues appropriate for the work quality?
6. Recommendations: specific actions to move each paper forward
```
