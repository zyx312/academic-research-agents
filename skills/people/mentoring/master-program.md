# Master's Program Supervision Guide

## Purpose

This skill provides a comprehensive framework for supervising master's students
through a 2-3 year program, covering coursework planning, research development,
thesis writing, publication strategy, defense preparation, and career guidance.
It addresses both thesis-track and non-thesis (project/coursework) options.

---

## 1. Program Timeline Overview

### Year 1: Foundation Building

| Semester | Focus | Key Milestones |
|----------|-------|----------------|
| Fall (Year 1) | Coursework + lab orientation | Join research group, attend seminars, identify advisor |
| Spring (Year 1) | Coursework + exploratory research | Complete core courses, begin literature review, form committee |
| Summer 1 | Intensive research or internship | First research prototype or pilot study |

### Year 2: Research Execution

| Semester | Focus | Key Milestones |
|----------|-------|----------------|
| Fall (Year 2) | Research + remaining courses | Thesis proposal defense, submit first paper draft |
| Spring (Year 2) | Deep research + writing | Complete experiments, begin thesis writing |
| Summer 2 | Thesis completion | Final experiments, full thesis draft |

### Year 3 (if applicable): Completion and Transition

| Semester | Focus | Key Milestones |
|----------|-------|----------------|
| Fall (Year 3) | Revisions + defense prep | Thesis revision, defense scheduling |
| Defense | Final milestone | Oral defense, revisions, submission |

### Critical Path Dependencies

```
Coursework completion --> Qualifying exam (if required)
                              |
Literature review --> Research question --> Proposal defense
                                                |
                                    Core research --> Results
                                                        |
                                              Thesis writing --> Defense
```

## 2. Coursework vs Research Balance

### Thesis Track

- **Typical credit split:** 60% coursework, 40% thesis research
- **Coursework strategy:** Front-load courses in Year 1; take only 1-2 in Year 2
- **Research ramp-up:** Begin attending group meetings and reading papers from Day 1
- **Key principle:** Every course project should, where possible, connect to thesis topic

### Non-Thesis Track (Project or Coursework Only)

- **Typical credit split:** 80% coursework, 20% capstone project
- **Capstone timing:** Begin in final semester; 3-6 month project
- **Career orientation:** Often better for industry-bound students
- **Publication:** Still possible but less expected

### Course Selection Strategy

1. **Core technical courses** (algorithms, statistics, domain-specific)
2. **Methods courses** (research methods, experimental design, data analysis)
3. **Breadth courses** (adjacent fields that enrich thesis perspective)
4. **Skills courses** (technical writing, presentation, project management)

Advisor should review course selections each semester and ensure alignment
with research direction and career goals.

## 3. Research Topic Identification

### Month 1-3: Exploration Phase

- Student reads 3-5 survey papers in advisor's field
- Attends all group meetings; reads papers presented by others
- Maintains a "questions and ideas" journal
- Has bi-weekly 1-on-1 meetings with advisor to discuss interests

### Month 3-6: Narrowing Phase

- Advisor provides 2-3 candidate research directions
- Student conducts focused literature review on each (20-30 papers per topic)
- Student presents a 15-minute "topic comparison" to advisor
- Joint decision on research direction based on:
  - Student interest and motivation
  - Advisor expertise and funding alignment
  - Feasibility within master's timeframe
  - Publication potential
  - Career relevance

### Month 6-9: Definition Phase

- Formulate specific research questions (2-3 for master's level)
- Define scope boundary: what is in and what is out
- Identify required datasets, tools, and collaborations
- Draft thesis proposal

### Topic Scoping Rules for Master's Level

A master's thesis should:
- Make a clear but bounded contribution (not paradigm-shifting)
- Be completable by one person in 12-18 months of focused work
- Produce at least one publishable result
- Demonstrate the student's ability to conduct independent research
- Build on existing methods or frameworks (not invent from scratch)

## 4. Publication Expectations

### Realistic Targets

| Program Duration | Minimum | Good | Excellent |
|-----------------|---------|------|-----------|
| 2-year MS | 0-1 papers | 1 conference paper | 1 journal + 1 conference |
| 3-year MS | 1 paper | 1 journal paper | 2+ papers |

### Publication Strategy

1. **Target venue selection** (month 6): Identify 2-3 conferences and 1-2 journals
2. **Submission timeline** (month 9-12): Align experiments with conference deadlines
3. **Writing process:** Draft paper in parallel with thesis chapters 3-4
4. **Advisor role:** Co-author; provide 2 rounds of detailed feedback
5. **Rejection handling:** Plan for at least one revision-and-resubmit cycle

### From Thesis Chapter to Paper

The most efficient path is to write thesis chapters as standalone papers:
- Chapter 3 (methodology) + Chapter 4 (results) = conference paper
- Expanded analysis + additional experiments = journal paper
- This avoids duplicate writing effort

## 5. Thesis Writing Process

### Structure (Engineering/CS Master's Thesis)

```
Abstract (300-500 words)

Chapter 1: Introduction (8-12 pages)
  1.1 Background and motivation
  1.2 Problem statement
  1.3 Research objectives and questions
  1.4 Contributions
  1.5 Thesis organization

Chapter 2: Literature Review (15-25 pages)
  2.1 Theoretical foundations
  2.2 State of the art (organized thematically)
  2.3 Critical analysis and research gaps
  2.4 Positioning of this work

Chapter 3: Methodology (15-25 pages)
  3.1 Overall framework / architecture
  3.2 Detailed method description
  3.3 Implementation details
  3.4 Experimental design

Chapter 4: Results and Discussion (20-30 pages)
  4.1 Experimental setup and baselines
  4.2 Quantitative results
  4.3 Qualitative analysis / case studies
  4.4 Ablation studies
  4.5 Discussion and interpretation

Chapter 5: Conclusion and Future Work (5-8 pages)
  5.1 Summary of contributions
  5.2 Limitations
  5.3 Future research directions

References (50-100 entries)
Appendices
```

### Writing Timeline (12-week plan)

| Weeks | Task | Output |
|-------|------|--------|
| 1-2 | Chapter 2 (literature review) | Draft with 50+ references |
| 3-4 | Chapter 3 (methodology) | Complete method description |
| 5-6 | Chapter 4 (results) | All tables, figures, analysis |
| 7-8 | Chapter 1 (introduction) | Motivation and framing |
| 9 | Chapter 5 (conclusion) | Summary and future work |
| 10 | Full draft assembly | Complete document |
| 11 | Advisor review and revision | Incorporate feedback |
| 12 | Final formatting and submission | Defense-ready document |

## 6. Defense Preparation

### Committee Formation

- Typically 3-5 members (advisor + 2-4 others)
- At least one member from outside the department (some programs)
- Form committee by end of Year 1 or early Year 2
- Committee approves proposal and evaluates defense

### Defense Format

- **Presentation:** 30-40 minutes covering all thesis chapters
- **Q&A:** 30-60 minutes from committee
- **Deliberation:** Committee meets privately to decide outcome

### Slide Deck Structure (35-40 slides)

```
1-2:   Title, committee acknowledgment
3-5:   Motivation and problem statement
6-8:   Background and related work (brief)
9-15:  Methodology (core contribution)
16-25: Results (key findings, visualizations)
26-28: Discussion and limitations
29-30: Conclusion and future work
31:    Publications resulting from thesis
32:    Acknowledgments
33-40: Backup slides for anticipated questions
```

### Common Defense Questions

- What is the main contribution of your thesis?
- How does your work differ from [specific related work]?
- Why did you choose method X over method Y?
- What would you do differently if starting over?
- How do your results generalize beyond this specific setting?
- What are the practical implications of your findings?

## 7. Career Guidance

### Industry vs PhD Decision Framework

| Factor | Favors Industry | Favors PhD |
|--------|----------------|------------|
| Motivation | Apply skills, earn well | Deep curiosity, academic career |
| Risk tolerance | Lower risk preferred | Can sustain 4-6 more years |
| Financial situation | Student debt, family obligations | Can sustain low stipend |
| Publication record | 0-1 papers sufficient | Already enjoys writing papers |
| Research independence | Prefers team/product focus | Wants to define own questions |
| Job market | Strong MS-level demand | Academic positions require PhD |

### Advisor's Role in Career Guidance

1. Have an explicit career conversation by end of Year 1
2. Connect industry-bound students with alumni and recruiters
3. For PhD-bound students: discuss potential programs, write strong letters
4. Provide honest assessment of student's research aptitude
5. Support internship opportunities during summer

## 8. Common Challenges and Solutions

### Challenge 1: Slow Start

**Problem:** Student spends too long on coursework, delays research.
**Solution:** Require joining lab meetings from Week 1; assign a small warm-up
project in the first semester that introduces tools and methods.

### Challenge 2: Advisor-Student Misalignment

**Problem:** Student wants to work on topic outside advisor's expertise.
**Solution:** Negotiate a topic that partially overlaps; consider co-advising
with another faculty member who covers the gap.

### Challenge 3: Perfectionism in Writing

**Problem:** Student polishes one chapter endlessly instead of completing all.
**Solution:** Enforce "complete rough draft first, polish later" approach;
set chapter-level deadlines with intermediate reviews.

### Challenge 4: Isolation

**Problem:** Master's students often feel less integrated than PhD students.
**Solution:** Include in all lab social events; pair with a PhD buddy;
encourage conference attendance even without a paper.

### Challenge 5: Scope Mismanagement

**Problem:** Student tries to do PhD-level work in a master's timeframe.
**Solution:** Clearly articulate master's-level expectations early; define
the "enough for a master's" threshold explicitly.

## Claude Prompt Templates

### Coursework Planning

```
You are an academic advisor helping a master's student plan their coursework.

Student profile:
- Program: [PROGRAM NAME, e.g., MS Computer Science]
- Research area: [AREA]
- Background: [UNDERGRAD MAJOR, key skills]
- Track: [thesis / non-thesis]
- Program duration: [2 / 3 years]
- Required credits: [N]
- Core requirements: [LIST]

Create a semester-by-semester course plan that:
1. Front-loads foundational courses in Year 1
2. Includes at least one methods/statistics course
3. Aligns electives with the research direction
4. Leaves adequate time for research in later semesters
5. Identifies courses where projects can serve thesis research

Output a table with semester, course name, credits, and rationale.
```

### Research Progress Evaluation

```
Evaluate this master's student's research progress and provide recommendations.

Student information:
- Program year: [1 / 2 / 3]
- Current semester: [Fall / Spring]
- Research topic: [TOPIC]
- Completed milestones: [LIST]
- Pending milestones: [LIST]
- Publication status: [none / submitted / published]
- Expected graduation: [DATE]

Assess:
1. Is the student on track for timely graduation?
2. What are the highest-risk items remaining?
3. Is the thesis scope appropriate (too broad / too narrow)?
4. What specific actions should be taken in the next 4 weeks?
5. Should the student consider adjusting their timeline or scope?
```

### Thesis Proposal Feedback

```
Review this master's thesis proposal and provide detailed feedback.

Proposal text:
[PASTE PROPOSAL]

Evaluate on these dimensions:
1. Problem clarity: Is the research question specific and testable?
2. Scope: Is this achievable in [N] months by one student?
3. Methodology: Is the approach well-justified and feasible?
4. Literature: Are key related works cited and positioned against?
5. Timeline: Is the proposed schedule realistic?
6. Expected contributions: Are they clearly stated and achievable?

For each dimension, rate as strong / adequate / needs improvement,
and provide 2-3 specific suggestions for strengthening the proposal.
```
