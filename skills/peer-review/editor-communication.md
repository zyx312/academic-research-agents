# Editor Communication

## Overview

Draft professional correspondence with journal and conference editors. Covers all stages
of the publication lifecycle: pre-submission inquiries, status follow-ups, responding to
decisions, appeals, withdrawal requests, and special issue proposals. Effective editor
communication can significantly impact publication outcomes.

## Types of Editor Communications

### 1. Pre-Submission Inquiry

Purpose: Confirm the manuscript fits the journal scope before investing effort in
formatting and submission.

**When to use:**
- Your topic is at the boundary of the journal's scope
- The journal has recently changed its focus or editorial team
- You want to gauge interest in a non-standard format (review, perspective, data paper)
- The manuscript is unusually long or structured differently

**What to include:**
- Concise description of the study (2-3 sentences)
- Key findings and their significance
- Why this journal is appropriate
- The manuscript type (original research, review, short communication)
- Do NOT attach the full manuscript unless requested

### 2. Status Inquiry

Purpose: Politely check on the progress of a submitted manuscript.

**Timing guidelines:**
- Wait at least 8-10 weeks after submission before inquiring
- For journals with stated review timelines, wait until that period has passed
- Check the online submission system first -- it may show the current status
- Send only ONE inquiry at a time; do not email multiple editors simultaneously

**What to include:**
- Manuscript ID and title
- Submission date
- A polite request for an update
- Offer to provide any additional information needed

### 3. Response to Editorial Decision

Types of decisions you may receive:
- **Accept**: Congratulations. Follow formatting instructions carefully.
- **Minor Revision**: Address all points; typically 2-4 weeks to respond.
- **Major Revision**: Substantial work needed; typically 2-3 months to respond.
- **Revise and Resubmit**: Near-rejection; major changes required with no guarantee.
- **Reject with encouragement**: Not suitable now but could be resubmitted after
  significant rework.
- **Reject (final)**: The paper will not be considered further at this venue.
- **Desk Rejection**: Rejected without peer review (scope mismatch, quality threshold).

### 4. Appeal of Rejection

Purpose: Request reconsideration of a rejection decision when you believe the
review process was flawed.

**Legitimate grounds for appeal:**
- Reviewer made factual errors that materially affected the decision
- Reviewer had a clear conflict of interest
- Key arguments or evidence were ignored or misunderstood
- The rejection contradicts the journal's published scope and criteria
- New data directly addresses the stated reason for rejection

**NOT legitimate grounds:**
- You simply disagree with the reviewer's opinion
- You believe your paper is better than other published papers
- You need the publication for promotion

**Timeline:** Most journals require appeals within 1 month of the decision date.

### 5. Manuscript Withdrawal

Purpose: Formally withdraw a manuscript from consideration.

**When appropriate:**
- You discovered a significant error in your work
- You have been offered acceptance at another journal (for non-overlapping submissions)
- The review process is taking unreasonably long
- Circumstances have changed (e.g., similar work published by others)

**Important:** Some journals require a formal withdrawal letter. Do not simply
submit elsewhere without withdrawing -- dual submission is an ethics violation.

### 6. Transfer Between Journals

Many publishers offer manuscript transfer between journals in their portfolio.

**Benefits:**
- Reviews may transfer, saving time
- Formatting may not need to change within the same publisher
- The editor may recommend a more suitable sister journal

**When to request:**
- After desk rejection with transfer suggestion
- When the editor recommends a more specialized journal
- When you realize a sister journal is a better fit

## Email Templates

### Pre-Submission Inquiry Template

```
Subject: Pre-Submission Inquiry - [Brief Topic Description]

Dear Professor/Dr. [Editor Name],

I am writing to inquire whether a manuscript entitled "[TITLE]" would be suitable
for consideration at [JOURNAL NAME].

Our study [1-2 sentences describing the research and key findings]. This work
is relevant to [JOURNAL NAME] because [1 sentence on fit with scope].

The manuscript reports [original research / a review / a perspective] and is
approximately [X] pages with [Y] figures. It has not been submitted elsewhere.

I would appreciate your guidance on whether this topic falls within the journal's
scope before preparing a full submission.

Thank you for your time and consideration.

Sincerely,
[Your Name]
[Affiliation]
[Email]
```

### Status Inquiry Template

```
Subject: Status Inquiry - Manuscript ID [MS-XXXX-XXXX]

Dear Editor,

I am writing to inquire about the status of our manuscript entitled "[TITLE]"
(Manuscript ID: [MS-XXXX-XXXX]), which was submitted on [DATE].

We understand the review process takes time and appreciate the effort involved.
If there is any additional information we can provide to facilitate the process,
please let us know.

Thank you for your attention.

Best regards,
[Corresponding Author Name]
[Affiliation]
```

### Appeal of Rejection Template

```
Subject: Appeal - Manuscript ID [MS-XXXX-XXXX]

Dear Professor/Dr. [Editor Name],

I am writing to respectfully appeal the decision on our manuscript entitled
"[TITLE]" (Manuscript ID: [MS-XXXX-XXXX]).

While we appreciate the reviewers' time and effort, we believe the decision
may have been influenced by [specific issue]:

1. [Factual error by Reviewer X]: The reviewer states [specific claim], but
   this is incorrect because [evidence/reference]. This error significantly
   affected the overall assessment.

2. [Misunderstanding of contribution]: Reviewer Y interpreted our method as
   [their interpretation], whereas our approach actually [correct description],
   as stated in Section [X] of the manuscript.

We have prepared a detailed point-by-point response addressing all reviewer
concerns, which we would be happy to provide. We believe that with the
opportunity to clarify these points, the manuscript merits reconsideration.

We respect the editorial process and will accept whatever decision you reach
after reviewing this appeal.

Sincerely,
[Corresponding Author Name]
```

### Withdrawal Request Template

```
Subject: Withdrawal Request - Manuscript ID [MS-XXXX-XXXX]

Dear Editor,

I am writing to formally request the withdrawal of our manuscript entitled
"[TITLE]" (Manuscript ID: [MS-XXXX-XXXX]) from consideration at [JOURNAL].

The reason for this withdrawal is [brief, honest explanation -- e.g., "we have
identified an error in our data analysis that requires significant rework"
or "we believe a more specialized venue would be appropriate"].

We apologize for any inconvenience this may cause to the editorial team and
reviewers, and we appreciate the time invested in processing our submission.

Sincerely,
[Corresponding Author Name]
```

### Requesting Expedited Review Template

```
Subject: Request for Expedited Review - Manuscript ID [MS-XXXX-XXXX]

Dear Editor,

I am writing regarding our manuscript "[TITLE]" (Manuscript ID: [MS-XXXX-XXXX]).

We respectfully request expedited review due to [legitimate reason]:
- Time-sensitive findings relevant to [current event/public health/policy deadline]
- Upcoming conference deadline where presentation depends on publication status
- Competing group working on similar findings (we can provide evidence)

We understand this is an exceptional request and will fully respect the editorial
team's decision. If expedited review is not possible, we are happy to proceed
with the standard timeline.

Thank you for considering this request.

Best regards,
[Corresponding Author Name]
```

## Understanding Editorial Decisions

### Reading Between the Lines

- "We encourage resubmission" in a reject letter = genuine interest; worth revising.
- "Does not meet our current priorities" = scope mismatch; try another journal.
- "We receive many excellent submissions" = generic rejection language; no signal.
- "The reviewers raised concerns that cannot be addressed" = fundamental flaw
  identified; major rethinking needed.
- "Consider a more specialized venue" = polite way of saying this is not high-impact
  enough for this journal.

### Handling Desk Rejection

Desk rejections (rejection without peer review) are common at high-impact journals
and happen for several reasons:
- Manuscript is outside the journal's scope
- Quality or significance does not meet the journal's threshold
- Topic is not of broad enough interest for the readership
- Formatting or language quality is below standard

**Response strategy:**
1. Do not take it personally -- top journals desk-reject 50-80% of submissions.
2. Read the editor's comments carefully for actionable feedback.
3. Consider whether the feedback suggests improvements before trying elsewhere.
4. Target a more appropriate journal -- use the editor's suggestions if offered.

## Professional Communication Principles

### Tone and Language

- Always be professional, respectful, and concise.
- Address editors by their proper title (Professor, Dr., Editor).
- Express gratitude for their time, even in difficult situations.
- Never be accusatory, emotional, or threatening.
- Keep emails under 500 words whenever possible.

### Timing

- Respond to revision requests promptly -- do not wait until the deadline.
- Submit revisions as early as possible to maintain momentum.
- Acknowledge receipt of decisions within a few days.
- For appeals, act within the stated window (typically 30 days).

### Record Keeping

- Save all correspondence with editors and reviewers.
- Keep a log of submission dates, manuscript IDs, and decision dates.
- Track revision deadlines and set reminders.
- Document which reviewer comments were addressed and how.

## Special Situations

### Editor is a Competitor

If you discover the handling editor works in a directly competing area:
- You may request a different handling editor (politely, with explanation).
- This is your right and editors generally accommodate such requests.

### Reviewer Misconduct

If you suspect a reviewer has violated confidentiality or has a conflict of interest:
- Report to the editor-in-chief (not the handling editor if they are the issue).
- Provide specific evidence, not just suspicion.
- The journal's ethics committee may investigate.

### Invited Revisions Taking Too Long

If you need more time for a revision:
- Request an extension BEFORE the deadline passes.
- Explain the reason briefly (additional experiments, collaborator availability).
- Most editors are flexible if you communicate proactively.

## Prompt Templates for Claude

### Draft a Pre-Submission Inquiry

```
Draft a pre-submission inquiry email for the following:
- Journal: [JOURNAL NAME]
- Editor: [EDITOR NAME, if known]
- Paper topic: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
- Key findings: [1-2 SENTENCES]
- Paper type: [original research / review / short communication]
- Why this journal: [REASON FOR FIT]

Keep the email under 200 words. Be professional and concise.
```

### Draft an Appeal Letter

```
Draft a professional appeal letter for a rejected manuscript:
- Journal: [JOURNAL NAME]
- Manuscript ID: [ID]
- Rejection reasons from editor/reviewers:
  [PASTE KEY REJECTION POINTS]
- Your counter-arguments:
  [LIST SPECIFIC FACTUAL ERRORS, MISUNDERSTANDINGS, OR NEW EVIDENCE]

The appeal should be respectful, evidence-based, and under 500 words. Focus on
factual errors or misunderstandings by reviewers, not on disagreements of opinion.
```

### Compose a Status Inquiry

```
Draft a polite status inquiry email for:
- Journal: [JOURNAL NAME]
- Manuscript ID: [ID]
- Manuscript title: [TITLE]
- Submission date: [DATE]
- Days since submission: [NUMBER]

Keep it brief (under 100 words), professional, and non-demanding.
```

### Analyze an Editorial Decision Letter

```
Analyze the following editorial decision letter and advise on next steps:
1. What is the actual decision (accept/revise/reject)?
2. What are the key issues raised?
3. Are the issues addressable?
4. What is the recommended course of action?
5. If revision is recommended, prioritize the changes needed.

Decision letter:
[PASTE DECISION LETTER]
```

### Draft a Revision Cover Letter

```
Draft a cover letter for a revised manuscript submission:
- Journal: [JOURNAL NAME]
- Manuscript ID: [ID]
- Original decision: [major revision / minor revision]
- Key changes made: [LIST OF MAJOR CHANGES]
- Number of reviewer comments addressed: [NUMBER]

The cover letter should summarize the major revisions, thank the reviewers,
and express confidence that the revised manuscript addresses all concerns.
```
