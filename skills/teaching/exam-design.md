# Exam Design Skill

## Overview

This skill supports the design of effective, fair, and pedagogically sound assessments for university courses. It covers assessment types, question design using Bloom's taxonomy, rubric creation, exam blueprinting, and emerging challenges in the AI era.

## Assessment Types

### Formative Assessment
- **Purpose**: Monitor student learning during instruction to provide ongoing feedback
- **Stakes**: Low or no stakes (not heavily weighted in final grade)
- **Timing**: Frequent, throughout the course
- **Examples**: In-class polls, minute papers, draft submissions, practice quizzes, peer review, concept maps, exit tickets
- **Key principle**: Results inform teaching adjustments, not just grading

### Summative Assessment
- **Purpose**: Evaluate student learning at the end of an instructional unit
- **Stakes**: High stakes (significant weight in final grade)
- **Timing**: At defined checkpoints (midterm, final, end of unit)
- **Examples**: Exams, final projects, research papers, capstone presentations, comprehensive portfolios
- **Key principle**: Measures achievement of stated learning objectives

### Diagnostic Assessment
- **Purpose**: Identify prior knowledge, skills, and misconceptions before instruction
- **Stakes**: No stakes
- **Timing**: Beginning of course or unit
- **Examples**: Pre-tests, concept inventories, background surveys, self-assessments
- **Key principle**: Informs course design and differentiation

## Question Design by Bloom's Level

### Level 1: Remember (Knowledge Retrieval)
- Multiple choice with factual recall
- Fill-in-the-blank with key terms
- Matching definitions to concepts
- True/false with justification

Stem patterns:
- "Define..."
- "List the characteristics of..."
- "Which of the following is..."
- "Identify the [term] that refers to..."

### Level 2: Understand (Comprehension)
- Explain a concept in your own words
- Summarize a process or argument
- Provide an example of a principle
- Interpret data from a chart or table

Stem patterns:
- "Explain how [X] relates to [Y]..."
- "In your own words, describe..."
- "Which statement best summarizes..."
- "Interpret the following graph/data..."

### Level 3: Apply (Use in New Situations)
- Solve a problem using learned procedures
- Apply a theory to a new scenario
- Calculate using given formulas and data
- Demonstrate a technique or method

Stem patterns:
- "Using [method], solve the following..."
- "Apply [theory] to explain [scenario]..."
- "Given the following data, calculate..."
- "How would you implement [concept] in [context]?"

### Level 4: Analyze (Break Down and Examine)
- Compare and contrast two frameworks
- Identify assumptions in an argument
- Distinguish between causes and correlations
- Deconstruct a complex system into components

Stem patterns:
- "Compare and contrast [X] and [Y]..."
- "What assumptions underlie [argument]?"
- "Analyze the relationship between..."
- "What evidence supports or contradicts..."

### Level 5: Evaluate (Judge and Justify)
- Critique a research methodology
- Assess the validity of an argument
- Justify a decision with evidence
- Prioritize solutions based on criteria

Stem patterns:
- "Evaluate the strengths and weaknesses of..."
- "To what extent is [claim] supported by..."
- "Which approach would be most effective and why?"
- "Critique the following [study/design/argument]..."

### Level 6: Create (Synthesize and Produce)
- Design an experiment or study
- Propose a solution to a novel problem
- Develop a framework or model
- Write an original argument or analysis

Stem patterns:
- "Design a [study/system/plan] that..."
- "Propose a solution to [problem] that accounts for..."
- "Develop a framework for analyzing..."
- "Construct an argument for or against..."

## Exam Blueprint (Specification Table)

An exam blueprint ensures systematic coverage of content and cognitive levels.

### Construction Steps

1. **List content areas** covered on the exam
2. **Assign relative importance** (percentage of exam) based on instructional time
3. **Determine Bloom's levels** appropriate for each content area
4. **Calculate number of items** per cell
5. **Verify alignment** with learning objectives

### Example Blueprint

| Content Area | Remember | Understand | Apply | Analyze | Evaluate | Total |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Topic A (30%) | 2 MC | 1 MC | 1 Short | 1 Short | - | 15 pts |
| Topic B (25%) | 1 MC | 2 MC | 1 Calc | - | - | 12.5 pts |
| Topic C (25%) | - | 1 MC | 1 Short | 1 Essay | - | 12.5 pts |
| Topic D (20%) | 1 MC | - | 1 Calc | - | 1 Essay | 10 pts |
| **Total** | 4 | 4 | 4 | 2 | 1 | **50 pts** |

### Cognitive Level Distribution Guidelines
- Introductory courses: 40% Remember/Understand, 40% Apply, 20% Analyze/Evaluate
- Advanced undergraduate: 20% Remember/Understand, 30% Apply, 50% Analyze/Evaluate/Create
- Graduate courses: 10% Remember/Understand, 20% Apply, 70% Analyze/Evaluate/Create

## Rubric Creation

### Analytic Rubrics
- Separate scores for each criterion
- More detailed feedback for students
- More time to create and use
- Best for complex assignments (papers, projects, presentations)

Structure:
| Criterion | Excellent (4) | Proficient (3) | Developing (2) | Beginning (1) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Criterion 1 | Description | Description | Description | Description |
| Criterion 2 | Description | Description | Description | Description |

### Holistic Rubrics
- Single overall score based on general impression
- Faster to create and apply
- Less detailed feedback
- Best for formative assessments or pass/fail decisions

### Rubric Design Principles
- Use observable, measurable language
- Define each level with positive descriptions (what students DO, not what they lack)
- Include enough levels to differentiate performance (typically 3-5)
- Pilot test with sample student work
- Share rubrics with students BEFORE the assignment
- Calibrate scoring with co-instructors or TAs

### Single-Point Rubrics
- List criteria and proficiency description only
- Space for comments on both strengths and areas for growth
- Less rigid, encourages qualitative feedback
- Good for developing student self-assessment skills

## Common Question Flaws to Avoid

### Multiple Choice
- **Implausible distractors**: All options should be plausible to someone who partially understands
- **"All of the above" / "None of the above"**: These test recognition, not understanding
- **Negative stems**: "Which is NOT..." confuses and tests reading skill, not content knowledge
- **Grammatical clues**: Ensure stem and all options are grammatically consistent
- **Absolute terms**: Avoid "always" or "never" as they signal incorrect answers
- **Longest option is correct**: Vary answer length across questions
- **Window dressing**: Unnecessary information that adds confusion without testing analysis
- **Double-barreled questions**: Test one concept per question

### Short Answer and Essay
- **Vague prompts**: "Discuss X" is too open; specify what aspect, how many points, what framework
- **Multiple sub-questions in one**: Number sub-questions and allocate points to each
- **No context or constraints**: Provide word limits, required elements, evaluation criteria
- **Recall-only phrasing**: "What is..." can be rephrased to higher levels: "Why does..." or "How would..."

### Calculation Problems
- **Unrealistic values**: Use plausible numbers from real-world contexts
- **No partial credit structure**: Define how points are allocated for setup, process, and answer
- **Missing units or significant figures guidance**: State expectations clearly
- **Only one correct path**: Design problems that reward understanding of multiple approaches

## Open-Book vs. Closed-Book Strategies

### Open-Book Exams
Best for:
- Courses emphasizing application, analysis, and evaluation
- Reducing test anxiety
- Testing ability to find, interpret, and apply information
- Professional contexts where reference materials are available

Design adjustments:
- Questions must require synthesis, not just lookup
- Time pressure naturally limits "search-everything" approaches
- Include novel scenarios that demand application of principles
- Require students to cite specific sources in their answers

### Closed-Book Exams
Best for:
- Foundational knowledge that must be automatized
- Gateway courses with essential prerequisite knowledge
- Professional licensing preparation
- Concepts that need to be recalled quickly in practice

Design adjustments:
- Provide necessary formulas, constants, or reference data
- Focus on understanding and application, not memorization of facts
- Consider a "crib sheet" policy (student-created reference page)

## Project-Based Assessment Alternatives

### Individual Projects
- Research papers with staged deliverables (proposal, draft, final)
- Design projects with documentation and presentation
- Portfolio assessment (curated collection of work over time)
- Case analysis with written report and recommendations

### Group Projects
- Define individual accountability within group work
- Include peer evaluation of contributions
- Require both group product and individual reflection
- Provide explicit collaboration rubrics

### Performance-Based Assessment
- Lab practicals and demonstrations
- Oral examinations (vivas)
- Teaching demonstrations (students teach a concept)
- Simulations and role-playing exercises

## Grading Calibration

### Ensuring Consistency
- Grade a random sample of 5-10 responses before grading the full set
- For multiple graders, establish inter-rater reliability sessions
- Grade one question at a time across all students (not one student at a time)
- Use blind grading when possible (student names hidden)
- Take breaks to avoid fatigue effects (grading drift)

### Grade Norming Process
1. Select anchor papers representing each quality level
2. Have all graders independently score the anchors
3. Discuss discrepancies and refine rubric language
4. Re-score a second set of anchors
5. Begin grading only when agreement is within 1 point

### Post-Exam Analysis
- Calculate item difficulty (proportion of students who answered correctly)
- Calculate item discrimination (correlation between item score and total score)
- Review distractors for each multiple-choice item
- Identify any items for revision or removal
- Compute reliability (Cronbach's alpha for internal consistency)

## AI-Era Assessment Challenges

### Designing AI-Resistant Assessments
- Use in-class, supervised assessments for critical evaluations
- Require personal reflection connecting course material to individual experience
- Design multi-step projects with checkpoints (proposal, draft, presentation, final)
- Incorporate oral components (defense, viva, interview)
- Ask students to analyze their own work process (metacognitive reflection)
- Use current events, local contexts, or course-specific data that AI cannot access

### Embracing AI as a Learning Tool
- Allow AI use with required documentation and critical evaluation
- "AI-assisted" assignments where students must improve upon AI output
- Comparative analysis: student solution vs. AI solution with critique
- Focus on skills AI cannot replicate: original research, ethical judgment, creative synthesis

### Policy Clarity
- Define "AI tools" explicitly (ChatGPT, Copilot, grammar checkers, translation tools)
- Specify allowed vs. prohibited uses per assignment type
- Require disclosure and citation of any AI assistance
- Explain the learning rationale behind restrictions

## Prompt Templates

### Create an Exam with Blueprint

```
Design a [midterm/final] exam for:
- Course: [title, level]
- Content covered: [list topics with relative importance]
- Duration: [minutes]
- Format preferences: [MC, short answer, essay, calculation, mixed]
- Target Bloom's distribution: [e.g., 20% Remember, 30% Apply, 30% Analyze, 20% Evaluate]

Please:
1. Create an exam blueprint (specification table)
2. Write [N] questions aligned with the blueprint
3. Provide an answer key with scoring rubric
4. Estimate time required per question
5. Include one or two higher-order "challenge" questions
6. Flag any potential issues (ambiguous wording, cultural bias, cognitive overload)
```

### Design a Rubric for an Assignment

```
Create a detailed rubric for:
- Assignment type: [research paper, presentation, lab report, project, etc.]
- Course: [title, level]
- Key learning objectives assessed: [list]
- Desired rubric type: [analytic, holistic, single-point]
- Number of performance levels: [3, 4, or 5]

The rubric should:
1. Include [N] criteria aligned with the assignment objectives
2. Provide clear, observable descriptions for each performance level
3. Use language that is constructive and forward-looking
4. Be suitable for sharing with students before the assignment
5. Include point allocations and total possible score
```

### Develop AI-Era Assessment Strategy

```
I teach [course] at the [level] level. My current assessments are:
[list assessments]

I am concerned about [specific AI-related concerns].

Help me:
1. Evaluate which current assessments are most vulnerable to AI misuse
2. Propose modifications to make assessments more AI-resistant
3. Suggest new assessment types that test skills AI cannot replicate
4. Draft a clear AI usage policy for each assessment type
5. Design one "AI-enhanced" assignment where students use AI as a tool and critically evaluate the output
6. Recommend strategies for maintaining academic integrity without creating an adversarial environment
```

### Post-Exam Item Analysis

```
Here are the results from my recent exam:
- [Question-level performance data or summary statistics]
- [Any student feedback or complaints about specific questions]
- [Grade distribution information]

Please:
1. Analyze item difficulty and discrimination patterns
2. Identify questions that should be revised or removed
3. Suggest improvements for problematic items
4. Evaluate whether the Bloom's level distribution was appropriate
5. Recommend adjustments for the next iteration of this exam
6. Comment on overall exam reliability and validity
```
