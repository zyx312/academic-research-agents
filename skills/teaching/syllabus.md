# Syllabus Design Skill

## Overview

This skill helps create effective, learning-centered syllabi that serve as both contractual documents and motivational tools. A well-designed syllabus communicates course expectations, inspires student engagement, and reflects intentional course design.

## Syllabus Philosophy: Contract vs. Learning Tool

### Rules-Centered (Traditional) Syllabus
- Focuses on policies, penalties, and prohibitions
- Tone: authoritative, sometimes adversarial
- Emphasizes what students must NOT do
- Effect: can create anxiety, positions instructor as enforcer

### Learning-Centered Syllabus
- Focuses on what students will learn, do, and become
- Tone: inviting, collaborative, professional
- Emphasizes growth, mastery, and student agency
- Effect: motivates engagement, positions instructor as guide

### Balanced Approach
The most effective syllabi combine both: clear expectations and policies alongside an inspiring vision of learning. The key is leading with the learning and embedding policies within a supportive framework.

## Essential Syllabus Components

### 1. Header Information
- Course title, number, section, CRN
- Semester and year
- Meeting times and location (building, room)
- Instructor name, title, pronouns (optional)
- Office location, office hours (multiple options including virtual)
- Contact information (email, preferred communication method)
- Response time commitment (e.g., "I reply to emails within 24 hours on weekdays")
- Teaching assistant information (if applicable)

### 2. Course Description and Rationale
- Official catalog description
- Your expanded description explaining WHY the course matters
- How it fits within the major/program curriculum
- Real-world relevance and applications
- Prerequisites and expected prior knowledge
- A welcoming statement that expresses genuine enthusiasm

Example framing: Instead of "This course covers X, Y, Z," try "By the end of this course, you will be able to think critically about [topic] and apply [skill] to solve real problems in [context]."

### 3. Learning Objectives
- 5-8 measurable objectives using action verbs (Bloom's taxonomy)
- Written from the student's perspective ("You will be able to...")
- Aligned with program-level outcomes
- Connected to specific assessments (show students the mapping)
- Include both disciplinary knowledge and transferable skills

### 4. Required Materials
- Textbooks (with ISBN, edition, availability options)
- Open Educational Resources (OER) when possible
- Software, tools, or equipment needed
- Estimated total cost of materials
- Library reserves, rental options, older edition compatibility
- Technology requirements (laptop, specific software, internet access)

### 5. Course Schedule

Design principles:
- Organize by week with clear topics, readings, and due dates
- Include learning objectives for each unit or module
- Mark exam dates, project deadlines, and holidays
- Build in flexibility (buffer weeks for catch-up or deeper exploration)
- Note "subject to change" with commitment to advance notice

Schedule format options:
- Weekly table: Date | Topic | Readings | Due | Objective
- Module-based: Group weeks into thematic units
- Calendar view: Visual representation for the full semester

### 6. Assessment and Grading

Components to include:
- Assessment types with weights (percentage or points)
- Rationale for each assessment (what it measures)
- Grading scale (letter grades, cutoffs, rounding policy)
- Late work policy (with or without penalty, grace periods)
- Resubmission or revision opportunities
- How grades are calculated and when they are posted

Example assessment distribution:
| Assessment | Weight | Learning Objectives | Due |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Participation/Engagement | 10% | All | Ongoing |
| Weekly Assignments (10) | 20% | 1, 2, 3 | Weekly |
| Midterm Exam | 20% | 1-4 | Week 8 |
| Research Project | 30% | 3, 5, 6 | Week 14 |
| Final Exam (cumulative) | 20% | All | Finals Week |

### 7. Course Policies

Essential policies:
- **Attendance**: Expectations, how absences affect grades, excused vs. unexcused
- **Late Work**: Deadline, penalty structure, extension request process
- **Communication**: Expected response times, how to address the instructor, email etiquette
- **Technology in Class**: Laptop/phone policy with rationale
- **Recording**: Whether class sessions may be recorded
- **Participation**: What counts as participation, how it is measured

### 8. Academic Integrity
- Define what academic integrity means in your course context
- Give specific examples of acceptable and unacceptable behavior
- Explain consequences with reference to institutional policy
- Address AI tool usage explicitly (what is allowed, what is not, and citation requirements)
- Provide positive framing: emphasize learning value of original work

### 9. Accessibility and Inclusion
- Disability services information and accommodation process
- Statement of commitment to inclusive learning
- How to request accommodations (with timeline)
- Universal Design for Learning (UDL) features already in the course
- Religious observance accommodation process
- Name and pronoun respect statement

### 10. Support Resources
- Academic support (tutoring centers, writing centers, library)
- Mental health and wellness resources
- Emergency contacts and safety information
- Title IX / harassment reporting
- Financial assistance or emergency funds
- Technology help desk

## Advanced Syllabus Design Strategies

### The Graphic Syllabus
- Visual map showing course structure and concept flow
- Helps students see the "big picture" at a glance
- Complements (does not replace) the text syllabus
- Use flowcharts, concept maps, or infographics

### The Liquid Syllabus
- Online, interactive version with multimedia
- Links to resources, videos, and detailed assignment descriptions
- Regularly updated (the "living document" approach)
- More accessible than a static PDF

### Specifications (Specs) Grading
- Each assignment is graded pass/fail against clear specifications
- Students choose which bundles of assignments to complete for their target grade
- Reduces grade anxiety, encourages revision and mastery
- Requires very clear rubrics and specifications in the syllabus

### Ungrading Approaches
- De-emphasize grades in favor of feedback and self-assessment
- Students propose their own grades with evidence
- Requires trust-building and explicit process description in syllabus
- Include detailed explanation of how final grades are determined

## Schedule Planning Strategies

### Backward Calendar Method
1. Start with final exam/project date
2. Work backward to place major assessments
3. Ensure adequate preparation time before each assessment
4. Add instruction and practice activities
5. Insert buffer days for review or catch-up

### Pacing Guidelines
- Introduce no more than 2-3 major new concepts per week
- Allow processing time between complex topics
- Front-load foundational concepts
- Schedule lighter weeks before heavy assessment periods
- Account for campus events, holidays, conference travel

### Reading Load Calibration
- Rule of thumb: 2-3 hours of outside work per credit hour per week
- Estimate actual reading time (not just page count)
- Vary reading difficulty across weeks
- Provide reading guides or focus questions
- Consider "reading burden audit" with colleagues

## Tone and Language

### Warm, Professional Tone
- Use "we" and "you" rather than exclusively "the student" or "one"
- Write in first person when describing your teaching philosophy
- Express genuine enthusiasm for the subject
- Acknowledge that learning can be challenging
- Avoid threatening or punitive language

### Inclusive Language Choices
- Avoid gendered language and assumptions about student backgrounds
- Use diverse examples and references
- Write for clarity across language proficiency levels
- Define jargon and discipline-specific terms

## Syllabus for Chinese Higher Education (中国高校教学大纲)

Additional components for Chinese university contexts:
- Course ideology integration (课程思政) elements
- Alignment with national education quality standards (国家质量标准)
- Bilingual course descriptions (if applicable)
- Credit system details (学时分配: lectures, labs, practice)
- Recommended reference textbooks (参考教材)
- Teaching method descriptions (讲授、讨论、实验、实践)
- Examination format (考试方式: 闭卷、开卷、课程设计)
- Grade composition details following university templates

## Syllabus Review Checklist

Before finalizing, verify:
- [ ] Every learning objective is assessed
- [ ] Every assessment maps to at least one objective
- [ ] All dates are correct (check against academic calendar)
- [ ] Required materials list is current and accurate
- [ ] Policies are consistent with department and institutional rules
- [ ] Tone is welcoming and professional
- [ ] Accessibility statement and resources are included
- [ ] AI usage policy is clearly stated
- [ ] Contact information and office hours are correct
- [ ] Schedule includes holidays and breaks
- [ ] Late policy and extension process are clear
- [ ] Total workload is reasonable (audit hours per week)
- [ ] Grading scale and calculation method are unambiguous

## Prompt Templates

### Generate a Complete Syllabus Draft

```
Create a comprehensive syllabus for:
- Course: [title and number]
- Level: [undergraduate/graduate], [year]
- Institution type: [research university, teaching college, etc.]
- Country/context: [for regulatory and cultural requirements]
- Credits: [number], meeting [days/times] for [duration]
- Enrollment: [expected class size]
- Prerequisites: [list]

Learning objectives: [paste or describe 5-8 objectives]
Assessment plan: [paste or describe]

Please generate a complete syllabus that:
1. Uses a learning-centered, welcoming tone
2. Includes all essential components (header, description, objectives, materials, schedule, assessments, policies, resources)
3. Creates a 16-week schedule with topics, readings, and due dates
4. Integrates clear policies on attendance, late work, academic integrity, and AI tools
5. Includes accessibility and inclusion statements
6. Adds a course rationale explaining why the subject matters
```

### Convert a Rules-Centered Syllabus to Learning-Centered

```
Here is my current syllabus:
[paste syllabus text]

Please revise it to be more learning-centered:
1. Rewrite the course description to emphasize relevance and student outcomes
2. Reframe policies in positive, supportive language (without removing necessary rules)
3. Add a welcoming statement and teaching philosophy paragraph
4. Connect each assessment to specific learning objectives
5. Add a "How to Succeed in This Course" section
6. Suggest accessibility and inclusion statements
7. Maintain all essential policy information while shifting the tone
```

### Design a Course Schedule

```
I need to plan a [N]-week course schedule:
- Course: [title]
- Topics to cover: [list major topics]
- Assessments: [list with target dates]
- Constraints: [holidays, conference travel, lab access, etc.]
- Textbook chapters: [if applicable]
- Class meetings per week: [number and duration]

Please create a detailed weekly schedule that:
1. Sequences topics logically with appropriate scaffolding
2. Distributes workload evenly (avoid bunching)
3. Provides adequate preparation time before assessments
4. Includes buffer weeks for review or catch-up
5. Notes readings, activities, and due dates for each week
6. Balances introduction of new material with practice and review
```

### Audit an Existing Syllabus

```
Please review this syllabus for completeness and effectiveness:
[paste syllabus]

Check for:
1. Missing essential components (compare against best practice checklists)
2. Alignment between objectives, assessments, and schedule
3. Tone analysis (welcoming vs. punitive)
4. Clarity of policies (ambiguity that could cause confusion)
5. Workload reasonableness (estimate hours per week)
6. Accessibility and inclusion considerations
7. Modern issues (AI policy, technology requirements)
8. Suggestions for improvement with specific rewording
```

### Create an AI Usage Policy Section

```
I teach [course name] at the [level] level. The course involves [types of assignments].

My philosophy on AI tools in this course is: [describe your stance]

Please draft an AI usage policy section for my syllabus that:
1. Clearly states which AI tools are permitted and for what purposes
2. Defines what constitutes acceptable vs. unacceptable use
3. Explains citation requirements for AI-assisted work
4. Provides the rationale for the policy (learning goals, not just rules)
5. Addresses specific assignment types in my course
6. Includes consequences for policy violations
7. Uses a tone consistent with a learning-centered syllabus
```
