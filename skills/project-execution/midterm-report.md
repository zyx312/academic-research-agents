# Research Project Midterm Report Guide

## Purpose

This skill provides a comprehensive framework for writing midterm progress reports
for funded research projects (NSFC, provincial funds, industry contracts, or
international grants). It covers report structure, progress-versus-plan comparison,
risk management, budget tracking, and strategies for presenting delays constructively.

---

## 1. Report Structure Overview

A midterm report typically follows this structure. Adapt headings to match
the specific funder's template.

```
1. Project Overview
   1.1 Project title and ID
   1.2 Principal investigator and team
   1.3 Funding period and total budget
   1.4 Report period

2. Objectives Recap
   2.1 Original objectives (as stated in proposal)
   2.2 Any approved modifications to objectives

3. Progress Against Plan
   3.1 Milestone-by-milestone status
   3.2 Detailed description of completed work
   3.3 Key findings and preliminary results
   3.4 Deviations from original plan and justification

4. Research Outputs
   4.1 Publications (published, accepted, submitted)
   4.2 Patents (filed, granted)
   4.3 Software, datasets, prototypes
   4.4 Presentations and invited talks
   4.5 Student training (theses completed, in progress)

5. Risk Assessment
   5.1 Technical risks identified
   5.2 Schedule risks
   5.3 Resource risks
   5.4 Mitigation strategies

6. Budget Expenditure
   6.1 Budget vs actual spending by category
   6.2 Explanation of significant variances
   6.3 Projected spending for remaining period

7. Next Steps
   7.1 Planned activities for remaining period
   7.2 Revised timeline if needed
   7.3 Expected deliverables

8. Appendices
   8.1 Publication list with copies or links
   8.2 Supporting data and figures
   8.3 Updated Gantt chart
```

## 2. Progress vs Plan Comparison

### Milestone Tracking Table

This is the core of the midterm report. Present a clear comparison:

| Milestone | Planned Completion | Actual Status | Completion % | Notes |
|-----------|-------------------|---------------|-------------|-------|
| M1: Literature survey | Month 3 | Completed Month 3 | 100% | On schedule |
| M2: Data collection | Month 6 | Completed Month 8 | 100% | 2-month delay (see 3.4) |
| M3: Algorithm design | Month 9 | In progress | 70% | Expected Month 11 |
| M4: Prototype system | Month 12 | Not started | 0% | Deferred to Q3 |
| M5: Evaluation | Month 15 | Not started | 0% | Dependent on M4 |

### Progress Visualization

Use a Gantt chart with two rows per milestone:
- Row 1: Planned timeline (original proposal)
- Row 2: Actual timeline (current status)

Color coding:
- Green: completed on time
- Yellow: completed late or currently delayed
- Red: significantly behind or at risk
- Gray: not yet started (as expected)

### Describing Completed Work

For each completed or in-progress milestone, provide:

1. **What was done:** Concrete description of activities and methods
2. **Key results:** Quantitative results where possible
3. **Significance:** Why this matters for the overall project
4. **Evidence:** Reference to publications, data, or demonstrations

## 3. Risk Identification and Management

### Risk Assessment Matrix

| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation Strategy | Status |
|------|------------|--------|---------------------|--------|
| Key personnel departure | Medium | High | Cross-train team members; document procedures | Active |
| Data quality insufficient | Low | High | Pilot study completed; quality metrics defined | Resolved |
| Equipment procurement delay | Medium | Medium | Alternative equipment identified; rental option | Monitoring |
| Scope creep from new findings | High | Medium | Change control process; advisory board review | Active |
| Publication rejection | Medium | Low | Target multiple venues; prepare revisions quickly | Ongoing |

### Technical Risks

- Algorithm does not converge or meet performance targets
- Sensor or equipment calibration issues
- Software dependency changes or deprecations
- Reproducibility challenges with complex systems

### Schedule Risks

- Student graduation or departure during critical phase
- Conference/journal review cycles longer than expected
- Dependency on external collaborators' timelines
- Regulatory approval delays (IRB, data access)

### Resource Risks

- Budget constraints limiting experimental scope
- Computing resources insufficient for planned experiments
- Lab access limitations (shared facilities, maintenance)
- Supply chain issues for specialized equipment

| Indirect | 50,000 | 25,000 | 25,000 | 50% (on track) |
| **Total** | **600,000** | **322,000** | **278,000** | **54%** |

### Variance Explanation Guidelines

When spending deviates significantly (more than 15%) from the proportional
expected amount, provide an explanation:

- **Under-spending:** Explain why and whether it indicates delayed activities
- **Over-spending:** Explain whether it is front-loaded by design or indicates
  a budget concern; describe adjustments planned
- **Category shifts:** If money moved between categories, note whether
  re-budgeting approval was obtained

## 5. Presenting Delays Constructively

### Framework for Discussing Delays

Delays are normal in research. The key is to present them honestly while
demonstrating active management:

```
1. Acknowledge the delay factually
   "Milestone M2 was completed 2 months behind the original schedule."

2. Explain the root cause
   "The delay was caused by [specific reason], which was
   [foreseeable/unforeseeable] because [context]."

3. Describe the impact
   "This has shifted the start of M3 by approximately 6 weeks."

4. Present the mitigation
   "To recover time, we have [specific actions taken], which will
   allow M3 to be completed by [new date]."

5. Show the revised timeline
   "The updated Gantt chart (Appendix A) reflects these adjustments.
   We remain on track for the final deliverable by [date]."
```

### Reframing Language

Instead of:
- "We failed to complete..." --> "We redirected efforts to address..."
- "The experiment did not work" --> "Initial results revealed that...
  leading us to refine our approach"
- "We are behind schedule" --> "We adjusted our timeline to accommodate..."
- "We underestimated the difficulty" --> "The complexity of [task] required
  additional investigation, yielding valuable insights about..."

### When Delays Are Severe

If the project is significantly behind (more than 3 months delay on
critical milestones), be direct and propose concrete recovery options:

1. **Scope reduction:** Drop non-essential objectives
2. **Timeline extension:** Request a no-cost extension
3. **Resource reallocation:** Shift budget or personnel
4. **Collaboration:** Bring in additional expertise
5. **Pivot:** Adjust research direction based on findings

## 6. Common Pitfalls in Midterm Reports

### Pitfall 1: Vague Progress Descriptions

**Bad:** "We made significant progress on the algorithm design."
**Good:** "We implemented three variants of the detection algorithm
(CNN-based, transformer-based, and hybrid) and benchmarked them on
the KITTI dataset, achieving 92.3% mAP with the hybrid approach."

### Pitfall 2: Missing Quantitative Evidence

Always include numbers: papers published, experiments run, accuracy
achieved, data points collected, students trained.

### Pitfall 3: Ignoring the Budget Section

Reviewers notice when budget reporting is thin. Even if spending is on
track, provide the comparison table and a brief narrative.

### Pitfall 4: No Updated Timeline

Always include a revised Gantt chart or timeline, even if the project
is perfectly on schedule. This demonstrates active planning.

### Pitfall 5: Listing Activities Instead of Results

A midterm report should emphasize what was learned and achieved, not
just what was done. Activities are means; results are the substance.

### Pitfall 6: Neglecting Student Outputs

Funded projects often expect student training outcomes. Report on
theses (completed and in progress), student publications, and skills
developed by team members.

## 7. Funder-Specific Considerations

### NSFC (National Natural Science Foundation of China)

- Use the official NSFC midterm report template
- Emphasize scientific findings and theoretical contributions
- List all publications with SCI/EI indexing status
- Report student training: number of PhD and MS students supported
- Budget must align with approved categories (equipment, travel, etc.)

### Provincial/Ministry Funds

- Focus on practical outcomes and applications
- Report technology transfer activities
- Include industry collaboration progress
- Emphasize alignment with provincial/national strategic priorities

### Industry-Funded Projects

- Focus on deliverables and milestones agreed in the contract
- Highlight IP generated and technology readiness levels
- Report on technology transfer and prototype status
- Budget reporting should align with contract payment schedule

---

## 8. Review and Quality Checklist

Before submitting the midterm report:

- [ ] All original objectives are addressed (even if modified)
- [ ] Milestone table shows clear planned vs actual comparison
- [ ] Key results are supported by data, figures, or references
- [ ] Budget table is complete with variance explanations
- [ ] Risk assessment is included with mitigation strategies
- [ ] Publications are listed with complete citation information
- [ ] Student training outcomes are reported
- [ ] Revised timeline or Gantt chart is included
- [ ] The report reads as a narrative, not just a list
- [ ] Formatting matches funder's template requirements

```

Delayed milestone: [MILESTONE DESCRIPTION]
Original deadline: [DATE]
Actual or projected completion: [DATE]
Root cause: [EXPLANATION]
Impact on other milestones: [DESCRIBE]
Actions taken to recover: [DESCRIBE]

Write a 200-word explanation that:
1. States the delay factually without defensiveness
2. Explains the root cause clearly
3. Describes the impact on the project timeline
4. Presents specific mitigation actions
5. Reassures the reviewer that the project remains viable
```
