# Invention Disclosure

## Overview

Draft invention disclosure documents for university technology transfer offices (TTOs).
The disclosure is the critical first step in the patent process -- it captures technical
details, prior art context, potential applications, and inventor contributions. Filing
a proper disclosure before any public presentation or publication is essential to
preserve patent rights.

## What Is an Invention Disclosure?

An invention disclosure is a confidential document submitted to your institution's
technology transfer office that describes a new invention, discovery, or software
creation. It serves multiple purposes:

1. **Legal record**: Establishes the date of conception and the identity of inventors.
2. **Patentability assessment**: Provides the TTO with information to evaluate whether
   the invention is patentable and commercially viable.
3. **Decision support**: Enables the TTO to decide whether to file a patent application,
   seek licensing partners, or release rights to the inventors.
4. **Prior art baseline**: Documents the state of the art at the time of invention.

The disclosure is NOT a patent application. It is an internal document that precedes
and informs the patent filing decision.

## Timing: When to File

### The Golden Rule: File BEFORE Any Public Disclosure

Public disclosure of your invention starts a clock that can destroy patent rights:
- **United States**: 12-month grace period after public disclosure to file a patent.
- **China (CNIPA)**: 6-month grace period, but only for specific circumstances
  (academic conferences recognized by the government, first display at certain
  exhibitions). General publication does NOT qualify.
- **Europe (EPO)**: No grace period. Any public disclosure before filing destroys
  novelty absolutely.
- **PCT**: Follows the rules of each designated country.

### What Counts as Public Disclosure

- Published journal papers, preprints (arXiv, bioRxiv), or theses
- Conference presentations, posters, or abstracts (even in small workshops)
- Public seminars, invited talks, or webinars
- Grant proposals submitted to agencies (may be publicly accessible via FOIA)
- Conversations with industry partners without an NDA
- Social media posts, blog articles, or press releases
- GitHub repositories or open-source releases without prior patent filing

### Recommended Timeline

1. **Conception**: As soon as you realize you have something novel, start documenting.
2. **Reduction to practice**: When you have working results or a prototype.
3. **Disclosure filing**: Submit to TTO BEFORE any of the public disclosures above.
4. **TTO evaluation**: Typically 2-8 weeks for the TTO to assess and decide.
5. **Patent filing**: If approved, the TTO arranges patent counsel and filing.
6. **Publication**: Now safe to publish, present, or open-source.

## Disclosure Form Sections

### Section 1: Title of Invention

- Use a descriptive, technically accurate title (not a marketing slogan).
- Include key functional terms that distinguish this from prior art.
- Example: "Self-Calibrating LiDAR-Camera Fusion System for Adverse Weather
  Autonomous Driving" (not "Better Sensor Fusion").

### Section 2: Inventors

List ALL individuals who contributed to the conception of the invention.
For each inventor provide:
- Full legal name
- Citizenship and residency status (affects filing strategy)
- Institutional affiliation and department
- Role in the invention (conceived idea, developed algorithm, conducted experiments)
- Employment status (faculty, postdoc, graduate student, staff)
- Contact information

**Important:** Inventorship is a legal determination, not an authorship decision.
A person is an inventor if they contributed to the conception of at least one
claim in the eventual patent. Lab technicians who merely followed instructions
are typically not inventors. Conversely, someone who contributed a key idea but
did not write code or run experiments may be an inventor.

### Section 3: Funding Sources

List ALL funding sources that supported this work:
- Government grants (agency, grant number, program name)
- Industry contracts (company name, contract number)
- Internal university funds
- Fellowship or scholarship sources

This is legally required because:
- US government funding triggers Bayh-Dole Act obligations (government gets
  a royalty-free license; must be reported to the funding agency).
- Industry funding may have IP clauses in the contract that affect ownership.
- International funding may have similar requirements.

### Section 4: Description of the Invention

Write a clear, detailed description covering:

**Problem Statement:**
- What problem does this invention solve?
- Why are existing solutions inadequate?
- What is the practical significance?

**Technical Description:**
- How does the invention work? Describe the mechanism, method, or system.
- What are the essential components or steps?
- Use diagrams, flowcharts, or block diagrams to illustrate.
- Describe at least one concrete embodiment (specific implementation).

**Key Innovation:**
- What is genuinely new compared to existing approaches?
- What is the technical advantage (faster, more accurate, cheaper, more robust)?
- Quantify the improvement whenever possible.

### Section 5: Prior Art and Novelty

Describe what existed before your invention:
- Published papers (yours and others')
- Existing patents in the field
- Commercial products or methods
- Open-source implementations

For each prior art reference, explain:
- What it does
- How your invention differs
- Why your invention is not obvious given the prior art

### Section 6: Stage of Development

Indicate the current maturity level:
- [ ] Conceptual idea only (no implementation)
- [ ] Computer simulation or modeling results
- [ ] Laboratory prototype or proof of concept
- [ ] Working prototype tested in realistic conditions
- [ ] Field-tested or pilot deployment
- [ ] Ready for production or commercialization

### Section 7: Commercial Potential

- Who would use this invention? (target industries, applications)
- What is the estimated market size?
- Are there potential licensees or partners you are aware of?
- Does this replace an existing product/method or create a new category?
- What is the competitive landscape?

### Section 8: Publication and Disclosure History

List ALL prior disclosures (even partial):
- Papers published or submitted
- Conference presentations given
- Conversations with external parties (with or without NDA)
- Grant proposals mentioning this work
- Any media coverage or press releases
- Date and venue for each disclosure

### Section 9: Supporting Materials

Attach relevant documents:
- Technical reports or internal memos
- Lab notebooks or experimental logs
- Diagrams, CAD drawings, or schematics
- Software source code (relevant portions)
- Test data and performance benchmarks
- Photographs of prototypes

## Writing for Non-Specialists

TTO staff often have broad science backgrounds but may not be experts in your
specific subfield. Write accordingly:

### Principles

1. **Lead with the problem, not the solution.** Explain why the problem matters
   in practical terms before describing your technical approach.
2. **Use analogies.** Compare your system to familiar concepts when possible.
3. **Define jargon.** The first time you use a technical term, define it briefly.
4. **Quantify impact.** "50% faster than existing methods" is more compelling than
   "significantly faster."
5. **Include figures.** A well-labeled block diagram or flowchart communicates
   more than pages of text.

### Structure Suggestion

- Paragraph 1: Problem and its real-world impact (2-3 sentences)
- Paragraph 2: Current approaches and their limitations (2-3 sentences)
- Paragraph 3: Your solution -- what it does and how (3-5 sentences)
- Paragraph 4: Key advantages and results (2-3 sentences)
- Paragraph 5: Potential applications and market (2-3 sentences)

## University TTO Process

### Typical Workflow

1. **Inventor submits disclosure** to TTO (online form or document).
2. **TTO reviews** for completeness and assigns a case manager.
3. **Patentability assessment**: TTO (sometimes with external counsel) evaluates
   novelty, non-obviousness, and commercial potential.
4. **Decision meeting**: TTO decides to file, defer, or release rights.
5. **If filing**: TTO engages patent attorney; inventors work with attorney on
   claims and specification.
6. **Patent prosecution**: Attorney handles office actions with inventor input.
7. **Licensing/commercialization**: TTO seeks licensees or supports startup creation.

### Inventor Obligations

- Respond promptly to TTO and attorney requests.
- Review patent drafts for technical accuracy.
- Disclose any new prior art that comes to your attention.
- Report any public disclosures that occur after filing.
- Cooperate with assignment paperwork (transferring rights to the university).

### Rights and Revenue

Most universities share licensing revenue with inventors. Typical splits:
- Inventors: 30-50% of net revenue
- Department: 15-25%
- College/School: 15-25%
- University central: 15-25%

Check your institution's IP policy for specific terms.

## China-Specific Considerations (CNIPA)

- Chinese universities often have their own IP management regulations (知识产权管理办法).
- Disclosure forms may need to be in Chinese with specific formatting.
- The 6-month grace period is narrow -- only applies to presentations at government-
  recognized academic conferences or designated exhibitions.
- Utility model patents (实用新型) have lower thresholds and faster processing
  than invention patents (发明专利) -- discuss with TTO which type to pursue.
- Service inventions (职务发明) belong to the employer by default under Chinese law.
- Recent reforms allow universities to grant inventors ownership rights in certain
  cases (赋予科研人员职务科技成果所有权).

## Prompt Templates for Claude

### Draft an Invention Disclosure

```
Help me draft an invention disclosure document with the following information:

Invention title: [TITLE]
Inventors: [NAMES AND ROLES]
Funding sources: [GRANTS, CONTRACTS]

Technical details:
- Problem being solved: [DESCRIPTION]
- How the invention works: [TECHNICAL DESCRIPTION]
- Key innovation over prior art: [WHAT IS NEW]
- Current development stage: [CONCEPT / PROTOTYPE / TESTED]

Known prior art:
[LIST OF RELEVANT PAPERS, PATENTS, PRODUCTS]

Potential applications:
[TARGET INDUSTRIES AND USE CASES]

Write the disclosure in clear language accessible to a TTO officer who may not
be an expert in this specific field. Include sections for: title, problem
statement, technical description, novelty, prior art, development stage,
commercial potential, and publication history.
```

### Assess Patentability from Research Results

```
Based on the following research results, assess whether there is a patentable
invention and what form a disclosure should take:

Research summary: [DESCRIPTION OF WORK]
Key results: [FINDINGS]
Existing approaches: [WHAT OTHERS DO]
Our advantage: [HOW WE ARE BETTER]

Evaluate:
1. Is there likely patentable subject matter?
2. What aspects appear novel?
3. What would be the strongest claims?
4. Are there any obvious barriers to patentability?
5. What additional information should be gathered for the disclosure?
```

### Write Non-Specialist Summary for TTO

```
Rewrite the following technical description for a technology transfer officer
who has a general science background but is not an expert in [FIELD]:

Technical description:
[PASTE DETAILED TECHNICAL DESCRIPTION]

Requirements:
- Use plain language and define all technical terms
- Lead with the practical problem and its significance
- Explain the solution conceptually before details
- Quantify advantages where possible
- Keep to 300-500 words
```

### Check Disclosure Completeness

```
Review the following invention disclosure for completeness and identify any
missing information that should be added before submission to the TTO:

[PASTE DISCLOSURE DRAFT]

Check for:
1. All inventor information complete?
2. All funding sources listed?
3. Technical description sufficient for a non-expert?
4. Prior art adequately surveyed?
5. Novelty clearly articulated?
6. Development stage documented?
7. Commercial potential assessed?
8. All prior public disclosures listed?
9. Supporting materials referenced?
```
